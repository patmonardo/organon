# VAK 1.11

## Sanskrit

```sanskrit
विक्षिप्तचित्तकस्यापि योऽनुबन्धः शुभाशुभः ।
महाभूतान्युपादाय स ह्यविज्ञप्तिरुच्यते ॥
```

## IAST

```text
vikṣiptacittakasyāpi yo'nubandhaḥ śubhāśubhaḥ |
mahābhūtānyupādāya sa hyavijñaptirucyate ||
```

## Padaccheda

```text
vikṣipta-cittakasya api
yaḥ anubandhaḥ
śubha-aśubhaḥ
mahābhūtāni upādāya
saḥ hi avijñaptiḥ ucyate
```

## Word Meanings

| Sanskrit | Meaning |
|---|---|
| vikṣipta | distracted, dispersed |
| citta | consciousness, mind |
| anubandha | connection, persistence, consequence |
| śubha | wholesome |
| aśubha | unwholesome |
| mahābhūta | great element |
| upādāya | having appropriated, grounded upon |
| avijñapti | implicit determination, unrepresented persistence |

## Literal Translation

> Even for one whose consciousness is distracted, that persistence of consequence, whether wholesome or unwholesome, which is grounded upon the great elements, is called **avijñapti**.

## Philosophical Translation

> Even when explicit consciousness has become dispersed, the consequential persistence of a wholesome or unwholesome determination, grounded in the elemental field of existence, is called **implicit determination (avijñapti)**.

## Commentary

The traditional Abhidharma schools developed extensive disputes concerning the ontological status of **avijñapti**, often treating it as a peculiar form of "unmanifest matter." From the standpoint of a theoretical science of Dharma, this interpretation leads toward an infinite proliferation of hidden entities.

The verse itself suggests a simpler and more profound interpretation. **Avijñapti** denotes the persistence of a determination after its explicit representation has vanished. A conscious act may cease, yet its consequential determination remains operative.

```text
Explicit Determination
        ↓
Vanishing of Representation
        ↓
Persistence of Consequence
        ↓
Avijñapti
```

This concept is closely related to:

- the Yoga notion of **saṃskāra**,
- Fichte's notion of **Consequentia**,
- Hegel's concept of **mediation preserved in reflection**.

In this interpretation, **avijñapti** is not a mysterious invisible substance, but the **implicit persistence of determination within the field of Dharma**.
