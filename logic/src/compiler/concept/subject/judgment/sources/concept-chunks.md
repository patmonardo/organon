**SOURCE ANALYSIS PHASE 1: Planning Document**

**Purpose**: Systematic chunking methodology to yield meaningful Logical Operations.
The TopicMap helps check and improve understanding of Hegel through step-by-step analysis.

**Status**: Work in progress - refining methodology as we practice.

**Workflow Stage**: **SEED** → **UPGRADES**
- **SEED**: Initial chunking plan (current state) - good enough to form initial seed
- **UPGRADES**: Refined chunks through actual study and editing
- As you study each chunk, edit and upgrade the seed chunks
- This document evolves from planning → studied/refined chunks

**Structure**: The Judgment of the Concept (375 lines)
- Introduction: Concept as ought, true adjudication, judgment of modality
- Concept from disjunctive judgment, diremption into extremes
- a. The assertoric judgment: Immediate, subjective assurance, passes to problematic
- b. The problematic judgment: Subject differentiated into ought and constitution, passes to apodictic
- c. The apodictic judgment: Truly objective, ground in constitution, transition to syllogism

**Note**: All mainline text is included in chunking, including examples (geometrical proofs, arithmetic, physics) even though they won't become Logical Operations. They are part of the systematic structure.

## Chunk 1: Introduction - Concept as Ought, True Adjudication (lines 4-19)

**Text**: Opening paragraph through "and is, or is not, in agreement with it"

**Key Points**:
- Judgments of existence ("rose is red") = hardly sign of great power
- Judgments of reflection = more propositions
- Judgment of necessity = subject matter in objective universality
- Judgment of concept = connection with concept found
- Concept at basis = ought to which reality may or may not conform
- First contains true adjudication
- Predicates: "good," "bad," "true," "right" = fact measured against concept as ought (presupposed), agreement or not

**Keywords**: judgments of existence, judgments of reflection, objective universality, connection with concept, ought, true adjudication, measured against concept, agreement

## Chunk 2: Judgment of Modality - Problematic, Assertoric, Apodictic (lines 21-56)

**Text**: "The judgment of the concept has been called" through "accidental, thought"

**Key Points**:
- Called judgment of modality (form of connection in external understanding, value of copula in connection with thought)
- Problematic: affirmation/negation optional/possible
- Assertoric: taken as true/actual
- Apodictic: taken as necessary
- Easy to go outside judgment, regard determination as merely subjective
- Concept (subjective) comes into play, relates to immediate actuality
- Subjectivity not to be confused with external reflection
- Concept emerged from disjunctive = opposite of mere mode/manner
- Earlier judgments subjective (abstract one-sidedness, concept lost)
- Judgment of concept = objective, truth (rests on concept in determinateness as concept, not external reflection or accidental thought)

**Keywords**: judgment of modality, problematic, assertoric, apodictic, external understanding, subjective, external reflection, mere mode, objective, truth, determinateness as concept

## Chunk 3: Concept from Disjunctive - Diremption into Extremes (lines 58-91)

**Text**: "In the disjunctive judgment" through "as the determinate connection of its moments"

**Key Points**:
- In disjunctive: concept posited as identity of universal nature and particularization (relation sublated)
- Concretion = simple result (must develop into totality, moments collapsed, not yet determinate self-subsistence)
- Shortcoming: objective universality attained completion in particularization, but negative unity only retreated, not determined as third moment (singularity)
- Result = negative unity = already singularity
- This determinateness must posit its negativity, part into extremes, conclude in syllogistic conclusion
- Proximate diremption = judgment in which unity posited (1) as subject (immediate singular), (2) as predicate (determinate connection of moments)

**Keywords**: identity, universal nature, particularization, sublated, concretion, totality, determinate self-subsistence, negative unity, singularity, diremption, extremes, syllogistic conclusion

## Chunk 4: Assertoric Judgment - Immediate, Ought and Constitution (lines 95-125)

**Text**: "The judgment of the concept is at first immediate" through "is still lacking"

**Key Points**:
- Judgment of concept at first immediate = assertoric judgment
- Subject = concrete singular in general
- Predicate expresses singular as connection of actuality/determinateness/constitution to its concept
- Examples: "This house is bad," "this action is good"
- Contains: (a) subject ought to be something (universal nature posited as self-subsistent concept), (b) particularity = constituted/external concrete existence (differs from self-subsisting universal nature, indifferent, may or may not conform)
- Constitution = singularity (escapes necessary determination of universal in disjunctive)
- Concrete universality from disjunctive divides into form of extremes (concept as posited unity connecting them still lacking)

**Keywords**: immediate, assertoric, concrete singular, connection, actuality, determinateness, constitution, ought, self-subsistent concept, external concrete existence, indifferent, conform, singularity, extremes

## Chunk 5: Assertoric - Subjective Assurance, External Third (lines 127-147)

**Text**: "For this reason the judgment is so far only assertoric" through "immediate abstract being"

**Key Points**:
- Only assertoric (credential = subjective assurance)
- Something good/bad/right/suitable hangs on external third
- Connectedness externally posited = still only in itself/internal
- When say something good/bad, don't mean only in subjective consciousness (may be predicates of object itself)
- Merely subjective character = implicitly present connectedness not posited yet (or only external)
- Copula still = immediate abstract being

**Keywords**: subjective assurance, external third, externally posited, in itself, internal, subjective consciousness, implicitly present, not posited, immediate abstract being

## Chunk 6: Assertoric Passes to Problematic (lines 149-164)

**Text**: "Thus the assurance of the assertoric judgment" through "the judgment is problematic"

**Key Points**:
- Assurance can be confronted by opposing one ("this action is good" vs "this action is bad" - equal justification)
- Subject = immediate singular (abstraction, does not have determinateness containing connection with universal concept)
- Contingent whether conformity to concept or not
- Essentially = problematic

**Keywords**: opposing assurance, equal justification, immediate singular, abstraction, determinateness, connection, contingent, conformity, problematic

## Chunk 7: Problematic Judgment - Subject Differentiated (lines 168-224)

**Text**: "The problematic judgment is the assertoric" through "a partition which is the judgment itself"

**Key Points**:
- Problematic = assertoric taken positively and negatively
- Particular judgment likewise problematic (positive and negative value)
- Hypothetical: being of subject/predicate also problematic
- Singular judgment and categorical = merely subjective
- In problematic as such: positing more immanent (content of predicate = connection of subject to concept, immediate as contingent present)
- Whether predicate ought to be coupled = problematic (indeterminateness falls on copula)
- Predicate = objective concrete universality (no determination to gain)
- Problematic element falls on immediacy of subject (determined as contingency)
- Must not abstract from singularity ("house or a house is good" vs "so indeed it is in the way it is made")
- Problematic element = moment of contingency, subjectivity of fact (contrasted with objective nature/concept, mere mode/constitution)
- Subject differentiated into universality/objective nature (ought) and particularized constitution of immediate existence
- Contains ground for being or not being what it ought to be
- Equated with predicate
- Negativity of problematic (implicates immediacy) = original partition into moments of universal and particular (already unity, partition = judgment itself)

**Keywords**: positive and negative, immanent, connection to concept, contingent, indeterminateness, copula, objective concrete universality, immediacy, contingency, singularity, moment of contingency, differentiated, ought, particularized constitution, ground, equated, original partition

## Chunk 8: Two Meanings of Subjectivity (lines 226-252)

**Text**: "One more comment that can be made is" through "one-sidedness of each when taken by itself"

**Key Points**:
- Both sides of subject (concept and constitution) could each be called subjectivity
- Concept = universal essence, withdrawn into itself, negative self-unity (constitutes fact's subjectivity)
- Fact also essentially contingent, external constitution (may be called mere subjectivity, contrasted with objectivity of concept)
- Fact = concept (self-negating unity) negates universality, projects into externality of singularity
- Subject posited as this duplicity
- Truth: two opposite meanings of subjectivity = in one
- Meaning of subjective become problematic (lost immediate determinateness, determinate opposition to predicate)
- Opposite meanings surface in ratiocination of ordinary reflection
- Subjectivity has no truth in one alone
- Duplicity = manifestation of one-sidedness of each taken by itself

**Keywords**: two meanings, subjectivity, universal essence, negative self-unity, contingent, external constitution, mere subjectivity, objectivity, self-negating unity, projects, externality, duplicity, in one, problematic, one-sidedness

## Chunk 9: Problematic Passes to Apodictic (lines 254-258)

**Text**: "When this problematic character" through "problematic but apodictic"

**Key Points**:
- When problematic character posited as character of fact (fact with constitution), judgment no longer problematic but apodictic

**Keywords**: problematic character, character of fact, constitution, apodictic

## Chunk 10: Apodictic Judgment - Truly Objective (lines 262-288)

**Text**: "The subject of the apodictic judgment" through "predicate of the apodictic judgment"

**Key Points**:
- Examples: "the house, as so and so constituted, is good," "the action, as so and so constituted, is right"
- Subject includes: (1) universal/what it ought to be, (2) constitution (contains ground why predicate does or does not pertain, whether subject corresponds to concept)
- Truly objective = truth of judgment in general
- Subject and predicate correspond, have same concept
- Content = posited concrete universality (two moments: objective universal/genus and singularized universal)
- Universal = itself and continues through opposite, universal only in unity with latter
- Universal like "good," "fitting," "right" = ought for ground, contains correspondence of existence
- Not ought or genus by itself, but correspondence = universality constituting predicate

**Keywords**: as so and so constituted, universal, ought, constitution, ground, corresponds, truly objective, truth, correspond, same concept, concrete universality, objective universal, genus, singularized universal, continues through opposite, unity, correspondence

## Chunk 11: Apodictic - Absolute Judgment on Actuality (lines 290-302)

**Text**: "The subject likewise contains" through "constitutes its soul"

**Key Points**:
- Subject contains two moments in immediate unity as fact
- Truth of fact = internally fractured into ought and being (absolute judgment on all actuality)
- Original partition = omnipotence of concept
- Equally turning back into concept's unity
- Absolute connection of "ought" and "being" = makes actual into fact
- Fact's inner connection (concrete identity) = its soul

**Keywords**: immediate unity, fact, internally fractured, ought and being, absolute judgment, actuality, original partition, omnipotence of concept, turning back, absolute connection, inner connection, concrete identity, soul

## Chunk 12: Ground in Constitution - Accomplished Copula (lines 304-325)

**Text**: "The transition from the immediate simplicity" through "connection of the subject to the universality"

**Key Points**:
- Transition from immediate simplicity to correspondence (determinate connection of ought and being, copula) lies in particular determinateness of fact
- Genus = universal existing in and for itself (appears unconnected)
- Determinateness = in universality reflected into itself but at same time into an other
- Judgment has ground in constitution of subject = apodictic
- Determinate and accomplished copula (hitherto abstract "is" developed into ground in general)
- Attaches to subject as immediate determinateness but equally connection to predicate
- Predicate = no other content than correspondence itself (connection of subject to universality)

**Keywords**: transition, immediate simplicity, correspondence, determinate connection, particular determinateness, genus, unconnected, reflected into itself, into an other, ground, constitution, accomplished copula, ground in general, immediate determinateness, connection, correspondence

## Chunk 13: Form of Judgment Passes Away (lines 327-344)

**Text**: "Thus the form of judgment has passed away" through "recovered in the whole"

**Key Points**:
- Form of judgment passed away: (1) subject and predicate in themselves same content, (2) through determinateness subject points beyond itself, connects to predicate, (3) connecting passed over into predicate (constitutes content, connecting as posited = judgment itself)
- Concrete identity of concept (result of disjunctive, inner foundation of judgment of concept, posited first only in predicate) = recovered in whole

**Keywords**: passed away, same content, determinateness, points beyond, connects, passed over, constitutes content, connecting as posited, concrete identity, inner foundation, recovered

## Chunk 14: Transition to Syllogism (lines 346-374)

**Text**: "On closer examination, the positive factor" through "the judgment has become syllogism"

**Key Points**:
- Positive factor for transition: subject and predicate each whole concept
- Unity of concept (determinateness constituting copula) = distinct from them
- At first stands on other side of subject (immediate constitution)
- But essence = to connect (not only immediate constitution but universal running through subject and predicate)
- Subject and predicate have same content
- Form of connection posited through determinateness of copula (determinateness as universal or particularity)
- Contains in itself both form determinations of extremes and determinate connection
- Accomplished copula, copula replete of content, unity of concept re-emerging from judgment (lost in extremes)
- By virtue of repletion of copula, judgment has become syllogism

**Keywords**: whole concept, unity of concept, determinateness, copula, distinct, immediate constitution, essence, to connect, universal running through, same content, form of connection, form determinations, extremes, accomplished copula, replete of content, re-emerging, repletion, syllogism
