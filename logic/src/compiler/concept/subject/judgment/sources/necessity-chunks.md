## Chunk 1: Introduction - Objective Universality (lines 4-24)

**Text**: Opening paragraph through "as genus and species"

**Key Points**:
- Universality advanced to = universality that exists in and for itself (objective universality)
- In sphere of essence corresponds to substantiality
- Distinguished from substantiality: belongs to concept, not only inner but posited necessity, distinction immanent
- Substance has distinction only in accidents (not principle within it)
- In judgment: objective universality posited (1) with determinateness as essential/immanent, (2) as diverse from it (particularity for which universality = substantial basis)
- Universality determined as genus and species

**Keywords**: objective universality, exists in and for itself, substantiality, posited necessity, immanent, principle, genus, species

## Chunk 2: Categorical Judgment - Genus Divides into Species (lines 28-58)

**Text**: "The genus essentially divides" through "reflected into its being-in-and-for-itself"

**Key Points**:
- Genus essentially divides/repels itself into species
- Genus only in so far as comprehends species under it
- Species only in so far as (1) exists in singulars, (2) possesses higher universality in genus
- Categorical judgment: predicate = universality in which subject possesses immanent nature
- First/immediate judgment of necessity
- Determinateness of subject (singular vs genus/species) = immediacy of external concrete existence
- Objective universality = first immediate particularization (determinate genus, not most proximate)
- What necessary = substantial identity of subject and predicate (distinguishing mark = unessential positedness/name)
- Subject reflected into being-in-and-for-itself

**Keywords**: genus divides, repels, comprehends, species, immanent nature, immediate judgment, external concrete existence, substantial identity, unessential positedness, being-in-and-for-itself

## Chunk 3: Categorical Distinguished from Positive/Negative (lines 59-84)

**Text**: "Such a predicate ought not to be classed" through "abstract, immediate being"

**Key Points**:
- Categorical predicate ought not classed with preceding judgments
- Example: "rose is red" vs "rose is a plant" (external property vs vegetable nature)
- Categorical distinguished from positive/negative judgment
- In positive/negative: singular accidental content
- In categorical: totality of form reflected into itself
- Copula in categorical = meaning of necessity
- In positive/negative = abstract, immediate being

**Keywords**: external property, vegetable nature, singular accidental content, totality of form, reflected into itself, necessity, abstract immediate being

## Chunk 4: Categorical - Inner Necessity Passes to Hypothetical (lines 86-107)

**Text**: "The determinateness of the subject" through "into the hypothetical judgment"

**Key Points**:
- Determinateness of subject (particular vs predicate) = at first still contingent
- Subject and predicate not connected with necessity by form/determinateness
- Necessity = still inner one
- Subject = subject only as particular (possesses objective universality essentially in accordance with immediate determinateness)
- Objective universal in determining itself (positing in judgment) = connection of identity with repelled determinateness (essentially, not merely accidental)
- Through necessity of immediate being, categorical conforms to objective universality
- Passes over into hypothetical judgment

**Keywords**: contingent, inner necessity, essentially, not merely accidental, necessity of immediate being, passes over, hypothetical judgment

## Chunk 5: Hypothetical Judgment - "If A is, then B is" (lines 111-163)

**Text**: "If A is, then B is" through "the being of an other"

**Key Points**:
- "If A is, then B is" or "The being of A is not its own being but the being of an other, of B"
- Posited: necessary connectedness of immediate determinacies (not yet posited in categorical)
- Two immediate/externally contingent concrete existences (in categorical only one, subject)
- Since one external to other, other immediately external to first
- Contents of both sides still indifferent (proposition of empty form)
- Immediacy = self-subsistent, concrete being; but essential = connection (being = mere possibility)
- Hypothetical does not say A is or B is, only connectedness posited as existing
- Each extreme = being of an other
- Principle of identity: A only A, not B; B only B, not A
- Hypothetical: finite = its own being but equally not its own being (being of an other)
- In being: finite alters, comes to be other
- In essence: appearance (reflective shining, inner connection not posited)
- Concept: identity posited (existent = concrete self-identity, immediately within it = being of an other)

**Keywords**: necessary connectedness, immediate determinacies, externally contingent, indifferent, empty form, mere possibility, connectedness, being of an other, principle of identity, concrete self-identity

## Chunk 6: Hypothetical - Relations of Reflection, Indeterminate Form (lines 165-204)

**Text**: "The hypothetical judgment can be more closely determined" through "So it is the disjunctive judgment"

**Key Points**:
- Hypothetical determined as: ground/consequence, condition/conditioned, causality
- Substantiality in categorical (form of concept); connectedness of causality in hypothetical
- Relations recur but essentially only as moments of one and same identity
- Not yet opposed as singular/particular and universal (only moments in general)
- Shape more that of proposition (indeterminate form, as particular judgment indeterminate content)
- Determination of content does not conform to subject/predicate relation
- Being (since being of other) = unity of itself and other = universality
- But also only particular (determinate being, does not refer merely to itself)
- Not simple abstract particularity: through immediacy, moments differentiated; through unity, particularity = totality
- What posited = universality as concrete identity of concept (determinations no subsistence, only particularities posited in identity)
- So it is disjunctive judgment

**Keywords**: ground, consequence, condition, causality, moments, one identity, indeterminate form, unity, universality, particular, totality, concrete identity, disjunctive judgment

## Chunk 7: Disjunctive Judgment - "A is either B or C" (lines 208-243)

**Text**: "In the categorical judgment" through "the greater universal"

**Key Points**:
- Categorical: concept = objective universality and external singularity
- Hypothetical: concept manifests in externality (negative identity)
- Through identity, objective universality and external singularity obtain determinateness (posited in disjunctive)
- Disjunctive = objective universality posited in union with form
- Contains: (1) concrete universality/genus in simple form (subject), (2) same universality as totality of differentiated determinations
- "A is either B or C" = necessity of concept
- First: self-identity of extremes = same extent, content, universality
- Second: differentiated according form of conceptual determination (but determination = mere form because of identity)
- Third: identical objective universality reflected into itself (content with determinateness of form): simple determination of genus; developed in difference = particularity of species and totality (universality of genus)
- Particularity in development = predicate (contains whole universal sphere, articulation of particularity = greater universal)

**Keywords**: negative identity, union with form, concrete universality, genus, totality, differentiated determinations, necessity of concept, self-identity, conceptual determination, particularity, species, greater universal

## Chunk 8: Disjunctive - Positive and Negative Identity (lines 245-260)

**Text**: "Upon closer consideration" through "unity of determinate particulars"

**Key Points**:
- Genus = substantial universality of species
- Subject = B as well as C ("as well as" = positive identity of particular with universal)
- Objective universal maintains itself fully in particularity
- Species mutually exclude ("either or" = negative connection)
- In negative connection, just as identical as in positive
- Genus = unity as unity of determinate particulars
- If genus = abstract universality (judgments of existence): species = diverse, mutually indifferent
- But universality = not external (comparison/abstraction) but immanent to genus and concrete

**Keywords**: substantial universality, as well as, positive identity, mutually exclude, either or, negative connection, unity, determinate particulars, abstract universality, immanent, concrete

## Chunk 9: Empirical Disjunctive Without Necessity (lines 270-295)

**Text**: "An empirical disjunctive judgment" through "universality of the predicate"

**Key Points**:
- Empirical disjunctive = without necessity ("A is either B or C or D, etc.")
- Species found beforehand; completeness = only subjective
- "Either or" excludes every other species and excludes within itself entire sphere
- Totality has necessity in negative unity of objective universal (dissolved singularity, immanent simple principle of differentiation)
- Empirical species: differences in accidentality (principle external, not their principle, not immanent determinateness of genus)
- Not reciprocally connected according determinateness
- Yet by determinateness, species constitute universality of predicate

**Keywords**: empirical disjunctive, without necessity, found beforehand, subjective completeness, negative unity, simple principle, differentiation, accidentality, external principle, immanent determinateness, reciprocally connected

## Chunk 10: Contrary and Contradictory Concepts (lines 296-316)

**Text**: "Here is where the so-called" through "mutually exclude one another"

**Key Points**:
- Contrary and contradictory concepts find proper place (disjunctive = essential difference of concept posited)
- Find truth: contrariness and contradictoriness differentiated (both as contraries and contradictory)
- Species contrary: merely diverse (immediate existence, subsisting in and for themselves by virtue of genus)
- Species contradictory: exclude one another
- Each determination one-sided and void of truth
- In "either or": unity posited as truth (independent subsistence = principle of negative unity by which mutually exclude)

**Keywords**: contrary, contradictory, essential difference, differentiated, merely diverse, immediate existence, exclude, one-sided, void of truth, unity, independent subsistence, negative unity

## Chunk 11: Proximate Genus (lines 318-353)

**Text**: "Through the identity just demonstrated" through "differentiating them in the nature of the genus"

**Key Points**:
- Through identity (negative unity), genus determined as proximate genus
- Expression indicates mere quantitative difference (more or less determinations)
- Which truly proximate genus = contingent
- If genus = universal by abstraction: cannot form disjunctive judgment (contingent whether determinateness left, not displayed in species, contingent completeness)
- In categorical: genus abstract form (not necessarily proximate, external)
- But when genus = concrete, essentially determined universality: simple determinateness = unity of moments of concept (sublated in simplicity, real difference in species)
- Genus = proximate genus (species possesses specific difference in essential determinateness of genus, species have determination differentiating them in nature of genus)

**Keywords**: proximate genus, quantitative difference, contingent, abstraction, concrete, essentially determined, simple determinateness, unity of moments, real difference, specific difference

## Chunk 12: Identity from Aspect of Determinateness (lines 355-374)

**Text**: "What we have just considered" through "manifests in its determination its negative unity"

**Key Points**:
- Identity of subject and predicate from aspect of determinateness (posited by hypothetical)
- Necessity of hypothetical = identity of immediate and diverse things (essentially negative unity)
- Negative unity in principle separates subject and predicate but now posited as differentiated (subject = simple determinateness, predicate = totality)
- Parting = difference of concept
- Totality of species = this difference
- Reciprocal determination of disjunctive terms = difference of concept
- Concept alone disjoins itself, manifests in determination its negative unity

**Keywords**: aspect of determinateness, negative unity, separates, differentiated, simple determinateness, totality, difference of concept, disjoins, manifests

## Chunk 13: Concept's Own Progressive Determination (lines 375-399)

**Text**: "Of course, the species comes up" through "has not proceeded from it"

**Key Points**:
- Species considered only under aspect of simple conceptual determinateness (not shape proceeding from idea)
- Reality dropped in simple principle of genus but essential differentiation = moment of concept
- In judgment: concept's own progressive determination posits its disjunction (as concept determined in and for itself, differentiated into determinate concepts)
- Concept = universal (positive and negative totality of particulars) = immediately itself one of disjunctive members
- Other member = universality resolved into particularity (determinateness as determinateness, universality displays as totality)
- If disjunction not attained this form: proof disjunction not risen to determinateness of concept, not proceeded from it

**Keywords**: simple conceptual determinateness, shape, idea, essential differentiation, moment of concept, progressive determination, disjunction, positive and negative totality, resolved into particularity, not proceeded from it

## Chunk 14: Example - Color Disjunction (lines 400-422)

**Text**: "Color is either violet" through "in the element of nature or spirit"

**Key Points**:
- Example: Color either violet, indigo, blue, green, yellow, orange, or red
- Empirically: confusion and impurity apparent (barbarism)
- If color = concrete unity of light and darkness: genus has determinateness constituting principle of particularization
- One must be utterly simple color (holds opposition in balance, contained/negated in intensity)
- Relation of opposition must take place over against it
- Indifferent neutrality of opposition further added
- Taking mixtures/shades as genus = inconsiderate procedure (too little reflection even for empiricism)
- Not place to discuss different forms disjunction may assume in nature or spirit

**Keywords**: confusion, impurity, barbarism, concrete unity, light and darkness, principle of particularization, utterly simple, opposition in balance, indifferent neutrality, inconsiderate procedure

## Chunk 15: Judgment Itself Disjoined - Transition to Concept (lines 424-450)

**Text**: "In the first instance" through "risen to the judgment of the concept"

**Key Points**:
- Disjunctive judgment has members in predicate
- But judgment itself equally disjoined (subject and predicate = members of disjunction)
- They = moments of concept (posited in determinateness but at same time identical)
- Identical: (a) in objective universality (subject = simple genus, predicate = universal sphere and totality of moments), (b) in negative unity (developed connectedness of necessity, simple determinateness fallen apart into difference of species, species in difference have essential connection and self-identity)
- Unity (copula) = concept itself (concept as posited)
- Judgment of necessity risen to judgment of concept

**Keywords**: disjoined, members of disjunction, moments of concept, identical, objective universality, negative unity, developed connectedness, essential connection, self-identity, copula, concept as posited, judgment of concept
