## I. THE DIALECTICAL RESTORATION OF PRAJNA (BEING)

### Posit 1: The Second Negation of Prajna
* **Text Span:** Lines 1238-1250
* **KeyPoint:** Jnana (The Concept) is the restored Prajna (Being) through the negation of Dharma (Essence).
* **Claim:** Prajna (Being) undergoes its first negation into Dharma (Essence), rendering Prajna illusory. Jnana is the *second negation* (the negation of the negation), which restores Prajna, but restores it as infinite mediation and self-negativity.
* **Relation:** Prajna → (First Negation) → Dharma → (Second Negation) → Jnana (Restored Prajna).

### Posit 2: The End of Illusory Determinations
* **Text Span:** Lines 1251-1257
* **KeyPoint:** In Jnana, Prajna and Dharma no longer subsist as independent, illusory sides.
* **Claim:** Because Jnana sublates them both, Prajna and Dharma lose their independent determination. They no longer exist merely as a unity where each shines illusorily in the other. Jnana does not differentiate itself back into them.
* **Relation:** (Prajna ⇆ Dharma) → Sublated into Jnana.

### Posit 3: The True Artha (Meaning) of Substance
* **Text Span:** Lines 1258-1264
* **KeyPoint:** Jnana is the truth of the substantial relationship in which Prajna and Dharma achieve self-subsistence.
* **Claim:** The substantial identity (the unity of Prajna and Dharma) proves to be the true Artha (Meaning/Object). This identity is simultaneously a resulting positedness.
* **Relation:** Substance (Prajna + Dharma) → True Artha (Jnana).

## II. THE ACTION OF SUBSTANTIAL IDENTITY (KRIYA)

### Posit 4: Kriya as Substantial Identity
* **Text Span:** Lines 1265-1276
* **KeyPoint:** Substantial identity only exists as Kriya (Action/Negativity).
* **Claim:** Artha (Truth) is a Kriya consisting of Prakasa (Manifestation) and Krama (Sequence). In Jnana, absolute Prajna attains adequate reality because Kriya *is* the absolute Prajna.
* **Relation:** Substantial Identity = Kriya (Prakasa + Krama).

### Posit 5: The Self-Differentiation of Kriya
* **Text Span:** Lines 1277-1299
* **KeyPoint:** Kriya constitutes the inner difference of Jnana.
* **Claim:** The moments of Jnana are themselves the *entire* Jnana because Kriya is identical to Prajna-in-and-for-itself. However, the reality it initially drew from Substance is not yet its *own* free determination. It must posit its own reality freely.
* **Relation:** Kriya (as derived from Substance) → Kriya (as freely posited by Jnana).

## III. THE FORMAL CONCEPT (DHARANA)

### Posit 6: The Immediate, Inner Shape of Jnana
* **Text Span:** Lines 1300-1314
* **KeyPoint:** At first, Jnana is only implicitly Artha; it is merely inner (and thus merely outer).
* **Claim:** In its immediate shape, the moments of Jnana are fixed determinations. It appears as the sphere of mere Manas (Understanding/Reflection), where it does not yet exist in-and-for-itself but counts only as something subjective and posited.

### Posit 7: Dharana (The Formal Samadhi)
* **Text Span:** Lines 1315-1334
* **KeyPoint:** This immediate shape constitutes Dharana (the Formal Concept), making Samadhi a subjective thinking external to the subject matter.
* **Claim:** In Dharana, the determinations of Jnana are isolated and qualitative. However, their internal subjective Dharma (identity) sets them into a dialectical movement that sublates their separateness, advancing them toward totality.
* **Relation:** Isolated Determinations (Manas) → Dialectical Movement → Totality (Dhyana).

## IV. THE OBJECTIVE CONCEPT (DHYANA)

### Posit 8: Jnana Passing into Stithi
* **Text Span:** Lines 1335-1344
* **KeyPoint:** In Dhyana (The Objective Concept), Jnana sheds subjective externality and becomes Stithi (Existence).
* **Claim:** The Formal Jnana (Dharana) transforms into Stithi by the necessity of its Rupa (Form). It emerges from inwardness and passes over into objective existence, becoming the real Jnana.
* **Relation:** Formal Jnana (Dharana) → Objective Existence (Dhyana).

### Posit 9: The Submersion into Objectivity
* **Text Span:** Lines 1345-1364
* **KeyPoint:** Jnana's freedom in Dhyana is still immediate; it is submerged in the subject matter.
* **Claim:** Having become one with the subject matter, Jnana's differences are now objective determinations (Stithi). To regain its freedom, it must set its subjective form (Dharana) *over against* its objectivity, making their identity explicitly posited.
* **Relation:** Subjective (Dharana) ⇆ Objective (Dhyana).

## V. THE IDEA (SAMADHI)

### Posit 10: Samadhi as the Adequate Jnana
* **Text Span:** Lines 1365-1376
* **KeyPoint:** Samadhi is the absolute consummation where Jnana achieves freedom within Objectivity.
* **Claim:** Buddhi (Reason) is the sphere of Samadhi. Here, the self-unveiled Artha realizes absolute freedom because in the real objective world (Dhyana), it completely recognizes its formal subjectivity (Dharana), and vice versa.
* **Relation:** Dharana (Subjectivity) ⇔ Dhyana (Objectivity) = Samadhi (The Idea / Absolute Freedom).
