### §1. Immediate Judgment and the First Agreement of Concept and Reality

**Lines:** 1–37

**Distillation:**
The judgment of existence is the first and still immediate form in which the concept seeks the agreement of concept and reality. Here the singular is supposed to be raised into universality and the universal made actual in the singular, but this agreement is not yet truly achieved. Because the form is immediate, the first judgment is also qualitative and a judgment of inherence: the subject stands as the immediate and essential side, while the predicate appears as something merely founded in it.

**Structural Moves:**

- **First reality of judgment:** Concept and actuality are first brought together.
- **Immediate form:** The agreement is only supposed, not yet achieved.
- **Inherence:** The predicate is grounded in the immediate subject.

### §2. The Positive Judgment Is Internally Untrue

**Lines:** 38–336

**Distillation:**
The positive judgment says, in its pure form, that the singular is universal. Yet the subject as immediate singular does not correspond to the universality predicated of it, and the predicate as abstract universal does not correspond to the subject's concrete richness. The positive judgment therefore contains a contradiction between form and content. It expresses what judgment must mean, but it fails to realize that meaning. For this reason its truth cannot lie in remaining positive, but only in passing over into negation.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Formulation:** The singular is universal.
- **Mismatch of extremes:** Abstract universality and immediate singularity do not truly fit.
- **Inner contradiction:** Form and content undermine the positive judgment.
- **Transition:** Truth requires negation.

---

### §3. The Negative Judgment Produces Particularity

**Lines:** 337–699

**Distillation:**
The negative judgment arises because the positive judgment is not true. In negating the abstract universality of the predicate, it does not abolish universality altogether, but preserves the universal sphere while denying that this immediate predicate belongs to the subject. The result is particularity: the singular is not universal in general, but particular. Negation thus mediates the judgment and produces the first reflected determination, though still within the structure of existence.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Negation of abstract universality:** Only the immediate predicate is denied.
- **Universal sphere retained:** Negation does not collapse into mere non-being.
- **Particularity produced:** The singular is now mediated as particular.
- **Result:** Judgment begins to reflect into itself.

---

### §4. The Infinite Judgment Destroys the Mere Form of Existence-Judgment

**Lines:** 700–798

**Distillation:**
The infinite judgment is the truth of the negative judgment because it pushes negation to the point where the very form of ordinary judgment breaks down. In its merely negative expression it becomes nonsensical, since subject and predicate are connected where no positive conceptual relation remains. Yet positively considered, the infinite judgment restores the singular as singular and the universal as universal through negation of negation. Thus the judgment of existence sublates itself: the merely immediate relation of subject and predicate has been broken, and judgment is driven into reflection.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Breakdown of ordinary judging:** Mere negative connection becomes senseless.
- **Negation of negation:** Singular and universal are restored through self-return.
- **Sublation of immediacy:** Existence-judgment can no longer remain in its first form.
- **Transition:** The judgment of existence passes into reflection.
