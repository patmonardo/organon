### §1. Judgment Is the Concept Positing Its Determinations as Opposed

**Lines:** 6–29

**Distillation:**
Judgment is not an external operation performed on ready-made concepts. It is the concept's own act of determining itself by positing its determinations over against one another. What had previously appeared as a merely subjective abstraction is now understood as belonging to the concept itself. For that reason, judging is the other function of the concept: the concept no longer only holds its determinations inwardly, but sets them out as opposed terms.

**Structural Moves:**

- **No external act:** Judgment belongs to the concept itself.
- **Posited opposition:** The concept sets its own determinations against one another.
- **Result:** Judgment is the concept in the form of self-diremption.

### §2. Judgment Is the First Reality of the Concept

**Lines:** 31–49

**Distillation:**
Judgment is the first realization of the concept, because here the concept enters determinate existence. Its moments are totalities that subsist on their own and yet remain connected through the unity of the concept. This is the distinctive tension of judgment: the moments are indifferent to one another as self-subsistent sides, and yet they are also inseparable because each stands within the unity that judgment still implicitly contains.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Entry into existence:** Judgment is the concept's first reality.
- **Self-subsistent sides:** The moments stand apart as independent terms.
- **Implicit unity:** Their separation still rests within one conceptual whole.

---

### §3. Subject and Predicate Are First Only the Polarized Sides of This Diremption

**Lines:** 50–120

**Distillation:**
The two sides of judgment are subject and predicate, but at first they are indeterminate apart from the act of judgment itself. They stand only as the more determinate and the more universal side. This is why the subject, taken before its predicate, is at first merely a name, and why so many disputes about judgments remain merely verbal. What the subject is in truth is first said in the predicate, because the predicate expresses the universal or conceptual determination that gives content to what otherwise remains only nominal.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Initial indeterminacy:** Subject and predicate do not pre-exist with finished content.
- **Polarization:** One side is more determinate, the other more universal.
- **Predicate gives truth:** The subject receives conceptual content only in predication.

---

### §4. The Copula Expresses More Than External Combination

**Lines:** 121–178

**Distillation:**
Judgment does not merely place two terms next to each other. Its essential claim is expressed in the copula: the predicate belongs to the being of the subject. The connection is therefore not just grammatical but ontological. Yet this unity is still only a connection of self-subsistent extremes, not the fulfilled unity that has already returned into itself. Judgment is thus inherently unstable: it says identity, but only in the form of an as yet unfulfilled relation.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Ontological "is":** The predicate is said to belong to the subject's being.
- **More than grammar:** Judgment is not mere verbal combination.
- **Still incomplete:** The unity is asserted, but not yet fully posited.

---

### §5. The True View of Judgment Begins from the Concept, Not from External Combination

**Lines:** 133–227

**Distillation:**
Judgment may be viewed either from the self-subsistence of its extremes or from the originative unity of the concept. The first standpoint yields the ordinary picture of an external combination of a ready-made subject and a ready-made predicate. The second yields the true one: judgment is the concept's own originative division. This is why the usual definition of judgment as the combination of two concepts is inadequate. It deals only with dead representations and misses what is essential, namely the immanent division of the concept and the necessary inequality of subject and predicate.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Two standpoints:** External combination or originative conceptual division.
- **Ordinary error:** Ready-made terms are treated as merely linked from outside.
- **True result:** Judgment is the concept's own self-division.

---

### §6. Judgment Moves Through the Double Process of Raising the Singular and Actualizing the Universal

**Lines:** 229–365

**Distillation:**
In judgment, the singular subject is raised into universality, while the universal predicate descends into determinate existence. This double movement is the truth of the earlier logical transitions of Being and Essence. What earlier appeared as transition into another or reflective shining in another is now gathered into one act: the concept divides itself in such a way that singularity returns toward universality and universality becomes actual in singularity. For that reason, subject and predicate are not fixed roles. Each can bear the determination of in-itselfness or existence, and the judgment itself posits this fluidity.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Upward movement:** The singular is raised into universality.
- **Downward movement:** The universal becomes actual in the singular.
- **Truth of earlier logic:** Being and Essence are sublated into this one movement.
- **Role-reversal:** Subject and predicate are not fixed once and for all.

---

### §7. Judgment Is Driven Beyond Itself by the Contradiction of the Copula

**Lines:** 367–426

**Distillation:**
The connection that judgment asserts is itself a concept-determination, but in judgment that connection is not yet fully posited. The copula says that subject and predicate are identical, yet the form of judgment equally preserves them as distinct extremes. This contradiction is the motor of the whole chapter. Judgment cannot remain at rest, because the unity it asserts is precisely what it has not yet achieved. Its development is therefore not the acquisition of something foreign, but the unfolding of what was already there in the concept. This unfolding drives judgment into syllogism, where the missing mediating unity is finally posited.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Contradictory copula:** Judgment says identity while preserving separation.
- **Inner motor:** This contradiction forces movement.
- **Development as unfolding:** Nothing external is added.
- **Transition:** Judgment passes into syllogism.
