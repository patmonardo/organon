### §1. Reflection as the Concrete Middle of the Syllogism

**Lines:** 4–59

**Distillation:**
The syllogism of reflection begins where the qualitative syllogism has already sublated the abstract isolation of its terms. The middle term is no longer abstract particularity but the totality of the determinations: singularity, singularity expanded as allness, and the genus that unites singularity and universality. The conclusion's connection is now explicitly mediated and necessary, so each determination is no longer taken singly but only in relation to the others. Reflection thus gives the syllogism its first genuine determinateness of form, even though this unity is still only reflective, not yet the absolute identity of the concept.

**Structural Moves:**

- **From abstract to concrete middle:** The middle becomes the totality of determinations.
- **Concrete determinateness:** Each term is now posited only through relation to the others.
- **First reflective unity:** The middle embraces the extremes, but only as reflection.
- **Opening of Part B:** The first reflective figure is the syllogism of allness.

### §2. The Syllogism of Allness and the External Universality of Reflection

**Lines:** 63–116

**Distillation:**
The syllogism of allness perfects the understanding's syllogism, but only externally. Its middle is concrete rather than abstract, and because it contains singularity, it restricts predication to what belongs to the subject in its concrete totality. This is a real gain over the qualitative syllogism, where arbitrary middle terms allowed contradictory inferences. Yet allness is still only the external universality of reflection: it gathers singulars into universality from the outside, and the singular still persists within that universality as an immediate self-subsistent term. Reflection has therefore achieved only the first negation of immediacy, not yet the negation of the negation.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Concrete allness:** The middle now includes the subject as a totality.
- **Restriction of arbitrary predicates:** Not every abstract property can mediate the conclusion anymore.
- **External universality:** Universality still gathers the singulars only reflectively.
- **Incomplete negation:** Allness is not yet conceptual universality.

### §3. The Illusion of Allness: The Major Premise Already Contains the Conclusion

**Lines:** 118–152

**Distillation:**
The very perfection of allness turns out to be illusory. Since the middle term is “all singulars,” the subject already possesses the predicate that the conclusion is supposed to establish. The major premise therefore presupposes the conclusion instead of grounding it. Hegel's familiar example is decisive: “All humans are mortal” is only valid if each singular human, including Gaius, is already known to be mortal. The syllogism of allness thus collapses into a reflective semblance of inference, because its universality is only the summed externality of singulars.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Reflective illusion:** Allness looks complete but only repeats the conclusion.
- **Presupposed conclusion:** The major premise secretly depends on what it claims to prove.
- **Counter-instance logic:** A single exception would destroy the premise.
- **Collapse of the figure:** The inference is not genuinely mediating.

### §4. Allness Gives Way to Induction

**Lines:** 154–185

**Distillation:**
The result of the syllogism of reflection is now explicit within the syllogism itself: the major premise presupposes the conclusion. Reflection therefore reveals that the true basis of the inference is subjective singularity. The singular does not receive its predicate by means of the syllogism; rather, its immediate connection to the predicate is what supports the universal claim. Once this is seen, singularity must be posited openly as the middle term. The syllogism of reflection therefore passes from allness to induction.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Presupposition made explicit:** Reflection openly shows the circularity hidden before.
- **Subjective singularity exposed:** The real support of the inference lies in singulars.
- **Middle-term shift:** Singularity must now be posited as the middle.
- **Transition:** The next figure is induction.

### §5. Induction as the Syllogism of Experience

**Lines:** 189–252

**Distillation:**
Induction takes the form $U-S-P$: the middle is singularity completed as a collected allness. One extreme is a predicate common to all these singulars; the other is the immediate genus exhausted in the collection of singulars or species. This eliminates the formal defect of the second figure because the predicate is at least equal in extension to the subject, making the difference between singularity and universality externally indifferent. Induction is therefore not mere perception but the syllogism of experience: the subjective gathering of singulars into a genus and the objective attribution of a universal property to that genus.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Completed singularity:** The middle is the collected totality of singulars.
- **Equality of extension:** Formally, subject and predicate are now extensionally matched.
- **Experience rather than perception:** The genus is conjoined to a universal mark through gathered cases.
- **Objective meaning:** The genus acquires existence in a universal property.

### §6. The Bad Infinity of Induction and Its Hidden Presupposition

**Lines:** 254–320

**Distillation:**
Induction remains essentially subjective because its middle terms are singulars in their immediacy, collected by external reflection. Universality is only completeness, and therefore only a task, never a finished result. Induction repeats the bad infinity: singularity ought to be identical with universality, but the singulars remain immediate, so the unity sought remains only an endless demand. Yet induction presupposes what it cannot complete, namely that the genus is already in and for itself conjoined with its determinateness. It therefore rests, not on immediate singularity, but on an implicitly universal singularity. The truth of induction is thus a syllogism whose middle term is a singular immediately universal in itself: analogy.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Bad infinity returns:** Empirical collection never reaches completed universality.
- **Problematic conclusion:** The inference remains only probable or unfinished.
- **Hidden presupposition:** Induction assumes the genus is already essentially determined.
- **Truth of induction:** The singular must be immediately universal in itself.

### §7. Analogy: The Singular Taken in Its Universal Nature

**Lines:** 322–345

**Distillation:**
The syllogism of analogy has the schema $S-U-P$, but its middle term is no longer an abstract universal. It is a singular taken in its universal nature: a concreted term whose genus is immanent within it. Another singular, sharing that same universal nature, stands as the other extreme. The example of the earth and the moon shows the structure clearly: what is inferred is not from one isolated property, but from a shared nature. Analogy therefore advances beyond induction by treating universality as immanent in the singular.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Singular middle in a new sense:** The middle is a singular carrying its genus within itself.
- **Shared universal nature:** The extremes are bound through one immanent nature.
- **Analogy's gain:** Universality is no longer only externally collected.
- **Exemplification:** The earth/mood example displays this form.

---

### §8. The Defect of Analogy: Immediate Unity Still Governs the Middle

**Lines:** 346–474

**Distillation:**
Analogy should not be degraded into mere similarity reasoning. Its true problem lies elsewhere: the universal nature in the middle term is still united with singularity immediately. Because of this immediacy, it remains undetermined whether the inferred predicate belongs to the singular because of the genus or only because of the singular's particularity. The notorious quaternio terminorum issue is secondary; the deeper defect is that the genus is present only implicitly, not yet as a negativity that has made the singular's determinateness the genus's own determinateness. Analogy therefore still remains a syllogism of reflection.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Critique of superficial similarity:** Analogy is not just likeness-based guessing.
- **Real defect:** The singular and the universal are still immediately united.
- **Underdetermined predication:** It is unclear whether the predicate belongs through genus or particularity.
- **Remaining reflective status:** The genus is still only implicit in the middle.

---

### §9. The Second Negation and the Transition to Necessity

**Lines:** 475–542

**Distillation:**
Analogy reveals itself as burdened by the same structure it sought to overcome: its premise is already its conclusion. The syllogism therefore demands the sublation of singular immediacy in the middle term. What remains is the objective universal, the genus purified of immediacy. This is the second negation: the external universality of reflection returns into itself and becomes a higher universality existing in and for itself. The syllogism of reflection has therefore passed into the syllogism of necessity.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Premise-conclusion identity returns:** Analogy still presupposes its result.
- **Sublation of immediacy:** Singularity must be negated in the middle.
- **Higher universality:** Reflection's universality becomes objective and in-and-for-itself.
- **Transition:** Reflection yields to necessity.
