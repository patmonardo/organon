# Concepts - Distillation

Source: [concepts.md](concepts.md)

---

### TC1. The Analytic of Concepts Seeks a System, Not a Rhapsody

**Distillation:**
The Analytic of Concepts opens by refusing any accidental or merely empirical collection of concepts. Pure understanding must be treated as a self-subsisting unity whose concepts belong together in a system. Kant therefore defines the task not as ordinary conceptual clarification, but as an inquiry into the understanding itself as the birthplace of a priori concepts. The governing demand is completeness through principle: each concept must have its place, and the total field must be exhaustively ordered rather than roughly estimated.

**Structural Moves:**

- Pure understanding must be grasped as a systematic whole.
- The Analytic of Concepts researches the understanding itself, not just given concepts.
- Discovery of pure concepts must proceed by principle rather than by chance.

---

### TC2. Understanding Is Discursive and Reveals Itself as a Faculty for Judging

**Distillation:**
Kant then redefines understanding positively. It is not intuitive but discursive: it cognizes through concepts. Concepts are functions for gathering representations under a common unity, and they never relate immediately to objects except through judgments. Judgment is therefore the form in which the understanding's activity becomes visible. Because every action of understanding can be traced back to functions of unity in judgment, the understanding can be represented as a faculty for judging.

**Structural Moves:**

- Human understanding cognizes through concepts, not intuition.
- Concepts are grounded on functions of unification.
- Judgment is the mediate cognition of an object.
- The understanding can be mapped through the functions of judgment.

---

### TC3. The Table of Judgments Gives the Clue to the Categories

**Distillation:**
The clue to pure concepts is therefore sought in the table of judgments. Kant lays out the four titles of quantity, quality, relation, and modality, each with three moments, and adds clarifications where transcendental concerns require more precision than general logic usually does. Singular judgments must be preserved as distinct moments; infinite judgments matter because they limit the sphere of cognition; hypothetical and disjunctive judgments display rule, consequence, and community within cognition; modality concerns not content but the status of the copula in thinking. The table is not a mere logical taxonomy. It is the exhaustive display of the understanding's own forms of unity.

**Structural Moves:**

- The logical functions of judgment are exhaustively classifiable.
- Transcendental logic sometimes sharpens distinctions blurred in general logic.
- Relation and modality already show architectonic features that will matter for the categories.
- The table of judgments is the principled route to a table of pure concepts.

---

### TC4. Categories Arise from the Synthetic Unity of the Manifold

**Distillation:**
Once the Transcendental Aesthetic has supplied a manifold of pure intuition, the argument shifts from logical form to transcendental content. General logic merely analyzes given representations into concepts. Transcendental logic asks how a manifold is first synthesized into cognition. Synthesis runs through imagination, but cognition in the proper sense requires concepts that give this synthesis necessary unity. The decisive claim follows: the same function that gives unity to representations in a judgment also gives unity to the synthesis of the manifold in intuition. Expressed generally, this is the pure concept of understanding. Thus the categories arise as the original concepts of synthesis through which an object of intuition in general can be thought.

**Structural Moves:**

- Pure intuition provides a manifold that must be synthesized.
- Imagination effects synthesis, but understanding gives it conceptual unity.
- The same function operative in judgment also governs synthesis in intuition.
- Categories are pure concepts of the synthetic unity required for object-cognition.

---

### TC5. The Category Table Has Internal Architectonic Structure

**Distillation:**
Kant does not stop at listing the categories. He immediately remarks on the inner form of the table. The four classes divide into mathematical and dynamical families: the first concern objects of intuition, the second their existence and relation. Within each class the third category is not a cheap derivative but the result of a special act combining the first two. Community, for instance, must be understood through the reciprocal coordination already hinted at in disjunctive judgment. The table is therefore not just complete; it is internally alive, with ordered families and mediated third terms.

**Structural Moves:**

- The category table divides into mathematical and dynamical classes.
- The third category in each class arises through a special combining act.
- Community is clarified through the reciprocal whole of disjunctive division.
- The table has a genuine architectonic, not just a finished inventory.

---

### TC6. Predicables and Scholastic Transcendentals Must Not Inflate the Table

**Distillation:**
Kant finally protects the table from two kinds of inflation. First, there are pure derivative concepts, or predicables, which belong to a fuller family tree of understanding but do not count as original categories. Second, unity, truth, and perfection are not extra transcendental predicates of objects. They are only logical criteria of cognition's agreement with itself, transformed from the quantitative categories into formal rules for concept-use. The category table is therefore complete without scholastic supplementation. What belongs to cognition's logical self-consistency must not be mistaken for a new ontological title.

**Structural Moves:**

- Predicables are derivative concepts, not additional ancestral categories.
- Unity, truth, and perfection are logical criteria, not pure object-concepts.
- The category table is complete without scholastic extension.
- Formal criteria of cognition must not be mistaken for properties of things.
