## Main Thesis

Kant's critical transformation of logic can be read as a relocation and division of the old ontological science.

What older metaphysics called ontology is critically recoded in the CPR as Transcendental Logic or the Analytic of the Pure Understanding: a science of the origin, domain, and objective validity of a priori object-cognition under the restriction to possible experience.

What appears in Jasche as General Logic preserves formal structures inherited from that older ontological field, but also carries a second layer of material concerned with learned cognition, method, clarity, popularity, and the relation of concept to intuition. For that reason the General Logic side should not be treated as a purely homogeneous formal block from beginning to end.

The result is not two unrelated logics. It is one larger scientific field split into a stricter object-grounding science on the Transcendental side and a formally articulated, partially inherited, partially pedagogical-cognitive continuation on the General side.

---

## 1. Transcendental Logic Is Kant's Relocated Ontology

General logic abstracts from all relation to objects and concerns only the form of thinking. Transcendental logic begins exactly where that abstraction becomes too weak for science. It asks how pure representations can be applied a priori to objects, and how such cognition can have objective validity at all.

This is why the CPR can strip the old “proud name of ontology” from dogmatic metaphysics without simply discarding its task. The task is not abolished. It is relocated. Ontology is no longer the direct doctrine of things in general, but the critical science of the a priori conditions under which objects can be cognized by us at all.

In that sense transcendental logic is the stronger scientific logic. It does what the older ontological framework was trying to do, but now under critique and under the restriction to possible experience.

---

## 2. General Logic Retains Ontological Inheritance

Even after this relocation, Kant's General Logic does not become a merely empty calculus. Its formal materials still preserve the older ontological framework in a critically reduced form.

Concept carries content and extension. Marks function as grounds of cognition. Higher and lower concepts retain genus-species subordination. Judgment orders subject, predicate, and relation. Syllogism explicitly develops ground and grounded, condition and conditioned, and the search for the universal rule. These are not arbitrary pedagogical leftovers. They are the formal residues of a science that had long been articulated through ontological vocabulary.

This is why the Jasche material can feel as though General Logic has been abstracted out of ontology rather than built from nowhere. The right claim is not that General Logic simply is unrestricted ontology. The right claim is that it preserves ontological form-language and formal structure after the critical relocation of objective validity to Transcendental Logic.

---

## 3. General Logic Is Not Purely One Thing in Jasche

The Jasche line also preserves material that does not behave like strict General Logic in the narrow formal sense.

Life turns to definition, division, exposition, and method. It distinguishes scientific from popular method, and acroamatic from erotematic instruction. Cognition and Idea shift attention toward consciousness, clarity, truth, certainty, aesthetic distinctness, popularity, and the relation between intuition and concept. Idea in particular is saturated with the sensible-discursive split and with the problem of logical and aesthetic perfection.

This means the later regions of the General Logic family are better read as boundary material than as simple continuation of pure formal logic. They still belong to the larger logic field, but they do so as a learned-cognition and method side, not as the pure form-only canon with which General Logic officially begins.

So the Jasche “Introduction” is not an accidental preface. It names a real stratum of the material: a discussion of cognition, method, and pedagogical-scientific formation that does not fit neatly inside strict General Logic, even though it is transmitted beside it.

---

## 4. Sufficient Ground Does Enter Transcendental Logic, But Critically

Old ontology often wanted the principle of sufficient reason as a doctrine of things in general. Kant does not simply omit that principle from the critical science. Nor does he leave it only to a marginal appendix.

In the Analogies, Kant says explicitly that the principle of sufficient reason is the ground of possible experience. There the principle no longer functions as an unrestricted law of being in general. It functions as the rule by which temporal sequence becomes objectively determinable within one possible experience.

This is the decisive critical recoding. Sufficient ground remains, but only as a principle of objective time-determination for appearances. What old ontology sought dogmatically as a law of things, Kant reformulates transcendentally as a law of experience.

The appendix on reflection then adds the needed discipline. It does not first introduce sufficient ground. It prevents reflection-concepts and comparison-titles from being misread as if they licensed a positive ontology of things in themselves.

---

## 5. Reflection Marks the Limit of the Relocated Ontology

The reflection appendix clarifies why Kant's relocated ontology cannot be allowed to slide back into Leibnizian metaphysics.

Identity and difference, agreement and opposition, inner and outer, matter and form: these are not self-sufficient ontological predicates that can be applied without first determining the transcendental place of the representations involved. The entire appendix exists to stop the confusion of appearance with pure understanding and thereby to stop the production of false ontology.

So the relocated ontological science has a double character. It is stronger than old General Logic because it grounds objective validity. But it is also narrower than old ontology because every use of its principles remains bound to sensibility, schema, and possible experience.

---

## 6. Hegelian Consequence

In the derivative architectonic reading used in this repository, Hegel's Objective Logic can then be treated as the self-moving completion of the field that Kant still holds apart.

Kant relocates ontology into Transcendental Logic, while leaving General Logic as the formal and methodical articulation of discursive cognition plus a boundary-zone of learned cognition. Hegel radicalizes the situation by turning what Kant still distributes across ontology-like transcendental science and formal logic into one immanent science of objectivity.

That is why the strongest bridge to Hegel does not run from Jasche General Logic taken in isolation. It runs from the Kantian split itself. Transcendental Logic is the stronger scientific side. General Logic preserves the formal articulation and pedagogical-cognitive aftermath. Hegel's Objective Logic reads, in that derivative sense, as the attempt to gather the whole field into one self-developing science.

---

## 7. General Logic as Broken Image of Science

On the strongest derivative reading used here, Jasche's General Logic is not yet a successfully constituted science but a broken image of one. It reaches toward science by demanding system, method, and rule-governed order, yet the principles and laws that should generate the science do not immanently organize the exposition. Moving those laws into a methodology of cognition does not repair the defect; it only relocates it. The genuinely scientific side appears instead in Transcendental Logic, where the principles and laws of cognition are unfolded as conditions of possible experience, and where syllogistic mediation already presses from ground-grounded into condition-conditioned sequence. In that sense the material often treated as introductory belongs more properly to the transcendental side of logic, which is also why this split already pressures toward the Hegelian demand that the principles and laws of logic be identical with the structure of science itself.

---

## Compressed Thesis

Kant does not simply oppose General Logic to Transcendental Logic as two independent disciplines.

He relocates the old ontological ambition into Transcendental Logic, where it becomes a critical science of a priori object-cognition under the restriction to possible experience.

General Logic then survives in a mixed form: partly as the formal residue of the older ontological framework, partly as a doctrine of learned cognition, method, and perfection that Jasche often transmits under the heading of introduction or closure.

This is why the Kantian logic field already pressures toward a stronger science than traditional learned logic, while still falling short of Hegel's immanent doctrine of objectivity.
