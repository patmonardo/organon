## The Continuous Boundary Argument

### 1. The boundary begins at schematism, not only at Ground

The argument does not wait until noumenon appears by name. It begins as soon as Kant asks how categories can have any objective validity at all. Schematism gives the answer: categories become meaningful for us only through a priori time-determinations that make them applicable to appearances. But the same act that gives them empirical significance also restricts them. Once the sensible condition is removed, the categories retain only the logical form of unity, not any determinate objective use. The first boundary is therefore already this: no schema, no object for us.

### 2. The System chapter turns that first boundary into the governing principle of the whole family

The System of Principles does not add a second doctrine beside schematism. It generalizes schematism's result. Synthetic a priori cognition is objectively valid only because the conditions of the possibility of experience are at the same time the conditions of the possibility of objects of experience. That means the whole system already stands under one rule: categories are valid only as principles of possible experience. The table of principles is therefore not an inventory of metaphysical powers. It is the ordered map of the empirical use of the categories.

### 3. The mathematical principles show what appearance must be like if an object is to be given at all

The first two families do not merely add content under the boundary; they articulate it positively.

The Axioms of Intuition show that appearance must be extensive magnitude. An object of intuition is given only through successive synthesis in space and time. This is why pure mathematics applies to appearances and why it cannot be detached from the forms of sensibility.

The Anticipations of Perception show that the real in appearance must have intensive magnitude or degree. Sensation as empirical matter is never anticipated, but whatever is sensed must admit a determinate more and less. So even quality enters experience only under a formal condition legislated a priori.

Taken together, these two principles say: if something is to count as appearance for us, it must be given under the measurable and gradable conditions of sensible intuition and perception. The boundary is therefore already operative at the level of what may count as an object of possible intuition.

### 4. The dynamical principles show what appearance must satisfy if it is to belong to one possible experience

The Analogies move from the possibility of isolated appearance to the possibility of connected experience. Persistence, causality, and community are not licenses for supersensible metaphysics. They are regulative principles of empirical use that determine duration, succession, and simultaneity only for appearances.

This is the decisive middle step in the continuous argument. The mathematical principles say what appearances must be like to be given. The dynamical principles say what they must satisfy to belong to one possible experience. The boundary is now stronger: not only must objects be sensible, they must stand in the lawful temporal nexus that makes experience one.

### 5. The Postulates make explicit what the earlier principles were already doing implicitly

The modality chapter states the restriction with unusual directness. Possibility, actuality, and necessity do not add new predicates to objects; they express only how objects stand in relation to cognition within experience. This is why the Postulates are rules of empirical use only, and why they bar transcendental use.

The refutation of idealism sharpens the same point architectonically. Even inner time-determination requires outer experience. The General Note then closes the family by saying openly what the sequence has been proving all along: all principles of pure understanding are principles only of possible experience. At this point the boundary is no longer merely structural; it is explicit doctrine.

### 6. Ground prevents the boundary from being misread as a hidden noumenal opening

Once the whole system has been restricted to possible experience, the next temptation is obvious: perhaps the categories still determine a second class of objects when intuition is removed. Ground blocks exactly that move. If the manner of intuition is left unspecified, what remains is only the transcendental object-thought, the indeterminate thought of an object in general. This is not yet a cognized noumenon.

So Ground clarifies the difference between transcendental significance and transcendental use. Categories may still point to the thought of an object in general, but they do not thereby determine any actual supersensible object for us. Noumenon is then admitted only negatively, as a boundary-concept that prevents appearance from being mistaken for thing in itself without opening any positive science of things beyond appearance.

### 7. Reflection cleans up the last illusion at the boundary

The appendix on reflection shows how thinkers generate false ontology when they forget the boundary that the whole Principles family has established. Leibniz's error is not merely one mistaken doctrine among others. It is the systematic confusion of the empirical use of understanding with a supposed transcendental use. Reflection-concepts are applied as if appearances were objects of pure understanding.

This is why the appendix returns to object in general in two critical registers. First, it revisits the transcendental object-thought already fixed in Ground. Second, it treats object in general as the highest problematic heading before the division into something and nothing. In both cases the point is negative: object in general is only a formal placeholder unless the mode of givenness is supplied. The appendix therefore does not open a noumenal science. It removes the last debris left by the temptation to think that it does.

## Compressed Thesis

The Principles family is one continuous boundary argument.

Schematism says that categories have empirical significance only through sensible time-determination.

The System chapter says that synthetic a priori validity belongs only to possible experience.

The mathematical and dynamical principles unfold the positive conditions under which appearances can be given and connected within one possible experience.

The Postulates state explicitly that modality and the whole system are of empirical use alone.

Ground blocks the slide from object in general to knowable noumenon.

Reflection diagnoses the metaphysical illusions produced when that restriction is forgotten.

So the family does not move from a constructive science of appearances to a second science of noumena. It moves from the valid empirical use of the categories to the disciplined closure of every attempted transcendental misuse of them.
