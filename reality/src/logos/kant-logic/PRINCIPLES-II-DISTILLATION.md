# Principles II - Distillation

Source: [principles-II.md](principles-II.md)

---

### TPII1. The Second Principle Anticipates Degree, Not Sensory Content

**Distillation:**
The Anticipations of Perception seem paradoxical at first because sensation, as the matter of experience, is never given a priori. Kant's claim is correspondingly precise: what can be anticipated is not which sensation will occur, but that whatever is real in appearance, insofar as it is sensed, must have an intensive magnitude, a degree. The understanding reaches the form of sensible filling, not the empirical quality that does the filling.

**Structural Moves:**

- Perception includes sensation as empirical consciousness.
- Sensation as matter cannot itself be given a priori.
- Yet whatever is real in appearance must be given with a degree.
- Anticipation therefore concerns intensive quantity, not empirical quality.

---

### TPII2. Sensation Has Magnitude Without Having Extensive Parts

**Distillation:**
Kant distinguishes intensive from extensive magnitude by the manner of apprehension. Sensation is not built up through a successive synthesis of parts to a whole in the way a line or duration is. It fills an instant. Yet it still admits more and less. From zero up to any given sensory presence there can be a generated increase of empirical consciousness, so the real of sensation has magnitude even though it is not extensive magnitude.

**Structural Moves:**

- Sensation is not spatial or temporal form.
- Its apprehension does not proceed from parts to whole.
- It nevertheless admits increase from 0 to any given measure.
- The real in appearance therefore has intensive magnitude.

---

### TPII3. Degree Implies a Continuous Passage Between Reality and Negation

**Distillation:**
Because every sensation can diminish, the real in appearance stands in a continuous nexus with negation. No degree is absolutely the smallest, and no transition from reality to its absence need be abrupt. Kant uses this to characterize intensive magnitude as unity apprehended at once, with multiplicity represented only through approach to zero. The doctrine of degree therefore builds continuity into sensible reality at the level of more and less.

**Structural Moves:**

- Every sensation can decrease.
- Between reality and negation lie indefinitely many intermediate degrees.
- Intensive magnitude is apprehended as a unity with gradational variation.
- Continuity therefore belongs to the real of appearance.

---

### TPII4. Appearance Is Continuous Both Extensively and Intensively

**Distillation:**
Kant then ties the two mathematical principles together. Space and time are continua as forms of intuition, and sensation is continuous as degree. Hence appearances are continuous magnitudes in both respects: extensively in intuition and intensively in perception. This allows him to distinguish a genuine quantum from a mere aggregate and to insist that interruption of synthesis yields only a collection, not one continuous appearance.

**Structural Moves:**

- Space and time are quanta continua.
- Sensory reality is continuous in degree.
- Appearance is therefore continuous as intuition and as perception.
- A quantum differs from a mere aggregate by continuity of synthesis.

---

### TPII5. Degree Blocks the Inference to Empty Space and Uniform Matter

**Distillation:**
One of Kant's main uses for the principle is negative-critical. If the real in appearance always has degree, then equal extensive spaces can be filled by different intensive degrees rather than by mixtures of fullness and emptiness. This undercuts the dogmatic inference from different densities or resistances to empty space. The principle does not prove a positive physical theory, but it removes the alleged necessity of a metaphysical one.

**Structural Moves:**

- Degree allows the same extension to be filled in different intensities.
- Absence of perception cannot prove absolute emptiness.
- Differences in matter need not be explained through voids.
- The principle loosens dogmatic metaphysical constraints on natural explanation.

---

### TPII6. Understanding Anticipates Only the Form of Sensible Variation

**Distillation:**
Kant closes by answering the worry that the understanding here reaches into what is merely empirical. Colors, tastes, and other sensory qualities are never anticipated as such. What is known a priori is only that any such reality, whatever its empirical content, must admit degree. The understanding therefore legislates the formal condition of sensible variation while leaving the qualitative matter of sensation to experience.

**Structural Moves:**

- Empirical qualities remain strictly a posteriori.
- The understanding does not predetermine which sensation occurs.
- It anticipates only that every sensation has degree.
- Experience remains necessary for all specific sensible content.
