# Principles III - Distillation

Source: [principles-III.md](principles-III.md)

---

### TPIII1. The Analogies Govern Existence Through Necessary Temporal Connection

**Distillation:**
The Analogies of Experience shift from the mathematical possibility of appearances to the dynamical determination of their existence. Experience is possible only if perceptions are connected necessarily, not merely juxtaposed contingently. Because time itself cannot be perceived, the existence of appearances in time must be determined through a priori rules of persistence, succession, and simultaneity. These analogies are therefore regulative principles of empirical use for existence in time rather than constitutive constructions of intuition or principles of transcendental use.

**Structural Moves:**

- Experience is cognition of objects through connected perceptions.
- Mere perception never yields necessity by itself.
- Time-relations must therefore be determined through a priori rules.
- The analogies are regulative principles for existence in time.

---

### TPIII2. The First Analogy Secures Duration Through the Persistence of Substance

**Distillation:**
The first analogy argues that all temporal determination presupposes something persistent in appearance. Since time cannot itself be perceived, there must be a substratum in appearances that represents time's enduring correlate. Kant identifies this with substance as the persistent real in appearance. Only on that basis can duration, simultaneity, and succession be determined at all. Substance is therefore not introduced here as a free metaphysical thing-in-itself, but as the enduring subject-position required for temporal objectivity within appearance. Nor is this yet the positive monistic One Substance. It is a presupposition reached by regressive critique: if experience of change is to be possible, something must persist as subject of alteration.

**Structural Moves:**

- Time itself is not an object of perception.
- Temporal determination therefore requires a persistent substratum in appearance.
- Substance is this persistent correlate of time.
- Substance functions as the enduring subject of changing predicates in appearance.
- The argument reaches substance regressively as a condition of experience, not progressively as the self-unfolding Absolute.
- Duration becomes possible only through relation to what persists.

---

### TPIII3. Alteration Belongs to States of Substance, Not to Substance's Own Arising or Perishing

**Distillation:**
Kant then draws a strict consequence from persistence. Genuine alteration is the succession of determinations in one and the same enduring subject. Arising and perishing, taken as origination or annihilation of substance within appearance, would destroy the very temporal unity needed for experience. What changes are states, accidents, and modes of existence of substance, not the existence of the substratum itself as appearance.

**Structural Moves:**

- Alteration presupposes an enduring subject.
- States can begin and cease while substance persists.
- Origination and annihilation of substance would fracture temporal unity.
- The first analogy therefore recasts change as change of determination.

---

### TPIII4. The Second Analogy Makes Objective Succession Dependent on Causality

**Distillation:**
The second analogy addresses the difference between merely subjective sequence in apprehension and objective succession in the object. The classic contrast is that the manifold of a house may be apprehended in any order, whereas the downstream movement of a ship cannot be reversed in apprehension if it is to count as one event. What makes the difference is a rule: the later state must follow the earlier one necessarily. That rule is causality. Only through it can an occurrence be represented as something that happens.

**Structural Moves:**

- Apprehension is always successive, but not every succession is objective.
- Some appearances permit reversible order in apprehension; genuine events do not.
- Objective succession requires a rule fixing what must come first and what must follow.
- The category of cause and effect supplies that necessity.

---

### TPIII5. Causality Also Grounds the Continuity and Order of Alteration

**Distillation:**
Kant extends the second analogy beyond simple event-ordering. If a state follows another in time, the transition cannot be a brute leap but must be generated through the continuous action of a cause across the time of alteration. The effect is therefore not produced all at once in a temporal vacuum. This gives the law of succession a dynamic content: experience requires not merely before-and-after, but rule-governed becoming.

**Structural Moves:**

- A cause determines the later state from the earlier one.
- The transition unfolds through the time of alteration.
- Alteration therefore proceeds continuously rather than by blind leap.
- Empirical temporal order becomes objectively valid only under causal law.

---

### TPIII6. The Third Analogy Makes Simultaneity Dependent on Interaction

**Distillation:**
The third analogy asks how coexistence can be objective. Reciprocal order in apprehension is not enough by itself, since time cannot be seen as a container in which things are simply placed together. For simultaneity to be cognized, substances must stand in community, that is, in reciprocal influence. Only interaction allows each to determine the other's temporal position within one shared time and thereby makes coexistence an object of experience.

**Structural Moves:**

- Simultaneity cannot be read directly off time itself.
- Reciprocal apprehension requires an objective ground.
- That ground is interaction or community among substances.
- Coexistence in one time is therefore cognizable only through reciprocal influence.

---

### TPIII7. The Analogies Complete the Temporal Constitution of Experience

**Distillation:**
Taken together, the three analogies determine duration, succession, and simultaneity as the basic temporal modes of experienced existence. They are not licenses for transcendental speculation about things in themselves, but rules under which appearances must stand if they are to belong to one possible experience at all. Persistence, causality, and community thus articulate the full temporal grammar of objects for us.

**Structural Moves:**

- The first analogy determines duration.
- The second determines objective succession.
- The third determines simultaneity.
- Together they secure the unity of one possible experience.
