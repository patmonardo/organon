# Principles IV - Distillation

Source: [principles-IV.md](principles-IV.md)

---

### TPIV1. The Postulates Concern the Relation of Objects to Cognition, Not New Object-Content

**Distillation:**
The Postulates of Empirical Thinking in General differ from the other principles because modality does not add a new predicate to the object. Possibility, actuality, and necessity express how an object stands in relation to our cognitive powers within experience. Kant therefore treats these principles as rules of empirical use only and, at the same time, as restrictions that bar the categories from any transcendental use.

**Structural Moves:**

- Modality does not augment the content of an object-concept.
- It expresses the object's relation to cognition.
- The postulates define possibility, actuality, and necessity only for empirical use.
- They therefore restrict as much as they authorize.

---

### TPIV2. Possibility Requires Agreement with the Formal Conditions of Experience

**Distillation:**
Kant's first postulate rejects the idea that non-contradiction alone suffices for objective possibility. A concept is objectively possible only if its synthesis agrees with the formal conditions of experience. This is why invented metaphysical notions, however contradiction-free, remain groundless when no experiential schema or lawful connection can be assigned to them. Objective possibility is therefore a matter of experiential form, not merely logical thinkability.

**Structural Moves:**

- Logical consistency is necessary but insufficient for possibility.
- Objective possibility depends on the form of possible experience.
- Pure concepts are valid only insofar as they structure that experience.
- Mere figments of the brain lack objective reality.

---

### TPIV3. Actuality Requires Perception or Lawful Connection to Perception

**Distillation:**
The second postulate states that actuality can never be read off from concept alone. Existence is known only through perception, or comparatively a priori through lawful connection with perception in one possible experience. Kant's example of magnetic matter makes the point: something may be actual without being directly sensed, provided its existence is warranted through empirical laws connecting it to actual perception.

**Structural Moves:**

- Existence is never contained in the mere concept of a thing.
- Perception supplies the mark of actuality.
- Actuality can also be inferred through lawful empirical connection.
- Even indirect actuality remains tied to possible perception.

---

### TPIV4. Necessity Is Only Hypothetical Within the Nexus of Experience

**Distillation:**
The third postulate narrows necessity just as sharply. No object of sense is known as absolutely necessary from concepts alone. Necessity in experience belongs only to the connection of states according to causal law. What is necessary is that, given the cause within one experience, the effect follows. Thus natural necessity is hypothetical, lawful, and intelligible, not blind fate and not metaphysical necessity of substances as such.

**Structural Moves:**

- Necessity cannot be derived from mere concepts of sensible things.
- It concerns lawful connection within experience.
- Causal dependence yields hypothetical necessity of effects.
- Modality remains confined to empirical nature.

---

### TPIV5. The Refutation of Idealism Grounds Inner Time-Determination in Outer Experience

**Distillation:**
Kant then turns the idealist challenge around. The issue is not how outer experience is inferred from an indubitable inner one, but how inner experience as temporally determined is possible at all. Since time-determination requires something persistent in perception, and nothing merely inner supplies that persistence, consciousness of my existence in time already implies actual things outside me. Outer experience is therefore not a doubtful inference from inner experience; it is the condition of inner experience's determinacy.

**Structural Moves:**

- Problematic idealism starts from allegedly immediate inner experience.
- Time-determination requires something persistent in perception.
- This persistence cannot be found in mere inner awareness.
- Outer experience is therefore necessary for determinate inner experience.

---

### TPIV6. The Postulates Are Called Postulates Because They State How Objects Are Given to Cognition

**Distillation:**
Kant also explains why these principles are called postulates. They do not enlarge the concept of the object but indicate the way the object is combined with our cognitive faculty. In that sense they resemble mathematical postulates, which do not prove an object but prescribe the synthesis through which it is given. The modal principles similarly specify the lawful mode in which an object can count as possible, actual, or necessary for us.

**Structural Moves:**

- Postulates are not dogmatic certainties without proof.
- Their legitimacy still requires deduction.
- They specify the object's relation to the cognitive faculty.
- The name marks their practical role in giving modal standing.

---

### TPIV7. The General Note Reaffirms That All Principles Concern Only Possible Experience

**Distillation:**
The concluding note widens the lesson of the whole system. Categories by themselves are mere forms of thought; their objective reality requires intuition, and for several relation-categories even outer intuition proves indispensable. This is why all synthetic principles of pure understanding are nothing more and nothing less than principles of the possibility of experience and therefore of empirical use alone. The entire system closes by binding necessity, once again, to appearances rather than to things in themselves.

**Structural Moves:**

- Categories alone do not yield synthetic cognition.
- Objective reality always requires intuition.
- Several core categories require outer intuition for their exhibition.
- The whole system of principles is therefore limited to possible experience.
