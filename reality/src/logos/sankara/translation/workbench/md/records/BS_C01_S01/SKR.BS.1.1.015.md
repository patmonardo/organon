### Source Text
महत ऋग्वेदादेः शास्त्रस्य अनेक विद्यास्थानोपबृंहितस्य प्रदीपव त्सर्वार्थावद्योतिनः सर्वज्ञ कल्पस्य योनिः कारणं ब्रह्म । न ही दृशस्य शास्त्रस्य ऋग्वेदादिलक्षणस्य सर्वज्ञ गुणान्वितस्य सर्वज्ञादन्यतः सम्भवोऽस्ति । यद्य द्वि स्तरार्थं शास्त्रं यस्मात्पुरुषविशेषात्सम्भवति , यथा व्याकरणादि पाणिन्यादेः ज्ञे यैकदेशार्थमपि , स ततो ऽप्यधिकतरविज्ञान इति प्रसिद्धं लोके । किमु वक्तव्यम् — अनेक शाखा भेदभिन्नस्य देव तिर्यङ्मनुष्यवर्णाश्रमादिप्रविभागहेतोः ऋग्वेदाद्याख्यस्य सर्व ज्ञानाकरस्य अप्रयत्ने नै व लीलान्यायेन पुरुषनिःश्वासवत् यस्मान्महतो भूतात् योनेः सम्भवः — ‘ अस्य महतो भूतस्य निःश्वसितमेतत् यदृग्वेदः ’ (बृ. उ. २ । ४ । १०) इत्यादिश्रुतेः — तस्य महतो भूतस्य निरतिशयं सर्वज्ञत्वं सर्वशक्तिमत्त्वं चेति ॥

## Translation

### Literal
Brahman is the source, the cause, of the great scripture beginning with the Rgveda - expanded by many seats of knowledge, illuminating all meanings like a lamp, and as if the product of omniscience. Such a scripture, marked by Rgveda etc., endowed with omniscient-grade content, cannot arise from anyone other than an omniscient source. In the world it is well known: from whatever special person a treatise of broad scope arises - even one with partial knowable range like grammar from Panini - that author is known to possess knowledge exceeding that scope. How much more here: this vast Vedic corpus, diversified into many branches and grounding distinctions of gods, animals, humans, social classes, and life-stages, treasury of all knowledge, arising effortlessly in playful manner like a breath from the Great Being - as sruti says, 'this Rgveda is the breath of that Great Being.' Therefore that Great Being possesses unsurpassed omniscience and omnipotence.

### Technical
Bhashya extends 1.1.2 with sastra-yonitva reasoning: Veda's scope and structure imply an omniscient omnipotent source, identified with Brahman.

### Interpretive Note
Scriptural authorship/cause argument reinforces Brahman's omniscience beyond cosmological inference alone.
