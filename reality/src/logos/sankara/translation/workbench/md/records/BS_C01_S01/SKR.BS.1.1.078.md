## Translation

### Literal
'Akasha, because of that indicator.' Here akasha should be taken as Brahman. Why? Because of that indicator: 'all these beings arise from akasha.' In Vedanta, origination of beings is delimited as from Supreme Brahman. True, element-space is shown as cause in the air etc. sequence; yet if the root cause Brahman is not taken, the restrictive expression 'from akasha alone' and universal qualifier 'all beings' become unsuitable. Likewise, 'into akasha they return' is a Brahman-indicator; and so are greatness and final resort. Unqualified greatness is taught only of Supreme Self ('greater than earth, greater than sky...'). Final resort too is more fitting in Supreme cause; sruti says Brahman is the supreme resort. Also, after criticizing Salavatya's finite view, Jaivali chooses akasha to teach the infinite and concludes: 'this is the highest udgitha; this is infinite.' That infinitude is a Brahman-linga. As for the claim that element-space first appears by familiarity: even if first cognized so, once Brahmanic indicators in sentence-end are seen, it is not retained. And akasha is elsewhere directly used for Brahman ('akasha is the bearer of names and forms...'), and even synonyms like vyoman and kha are used for Brahman in multiple texts. Therefore determining Brahman-sense from sentence-end is proper even when the word begins the sentence. Hence it is established: akasha here means Brahman. (22)
