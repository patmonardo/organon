## Translation

### Literal
Indra, the deity-self, seeing by scriptural vision 'I myself am Supreme Brahman,' taught accordingly: 'know me alone' - like the sage Vamadeva who realized 'I was Manu and Surya'; scripture says whoever among gods awoke thus became That. As for the objection that Indra praised himself by body-bound deeds like slaying Tvastar, this must be answered: those deeds are not introduced to glorify the knowable Indra as such ('since I did this, know me'), but to praise knowledge itself. For after recounting those daring acts he immediately follows with praise of knowledge: 'yet not even a hair of mine is harmed there; he who knows me, no world of his is harmed by any action...' Meaning: because even I, become Brahman, am untouched by such fierce acts, so too one who knows thus is not harmed by any action. The knowable referred to in 'I am prana, conscious self' is Brahman alone. Therefore this is Brahman-teaching. (30)

### Technical
Bhashya on 1.1.30 interprets Indra's self-reference as shastra-drsti identity-upadesha (Vamadeva model), with heroic episodes serving jnana-stuti, not deity-limitation.

### Interpretive Note
Mythic narrative is recast as pedagogical vehicle for Brahman-knowledge.
