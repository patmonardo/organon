## Translation

### Literal
Further, after 'higher than the imperishable is the highest,' and after stating creation of principles from prana down to earth ('from this are born prana...'), we see the text presenting a form of that very source of beings as the totality of modifications: 'fire is His head, moon and sun His eyes, directions His ears, Vedas His speech, wind His breath, the universe His heart, earth from His feet; this indeed is the inner self of all beings.' This is proper only to the Supreme Lord as cause of all transformations. Not to embodied self, whose body-glory is limited; nor to Pradhana, since inner-selfhood of all beings cannot apply. Therefore only the Supreme Lord is source of beings. How is this form-presentation known to concern source-of-beings? From context and from pulling forward the prior referent by 'this.' Having introduced source-of-beings, statements like 'from this is born prana' and 'this is the inner self of all beings' concern that same topic, just as after introducing a teacher one says, 'study from this one; this is master of Veda and Vedanga.' If asked how an invisibility-qualified source can have form-like description, no fault: the intention is to express all-selfhood, not corporeality, like 'I am food, I am eater of food.'

### Technical
Bhashya supporting 1.2.23 reads cosmic-body imagery as rupa-upanyasa of the previously introduced bhuta-yoni, secured by prakarana continuity and pronoun anukarsana; this suits Brahman-causality alone.

### Interpretive Note
Cosmic-form language is hermeneutic, signaling total immanence rather than finite embodiment.
