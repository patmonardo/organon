अत्राह साङ्ख्यः — ज्ञेयत्वावचनात् , इत्यसिद्धम् । कथम् ? श्रूयते ह्युत्तरत्राव्यक्तशब्दोदितस्य प्रधानस्य ज्ञेयत्ववचनम् — ‘ अशब्दमस्पर्शमरूपमव्ययं तथारसं नित्यमगन्धवच्च यत् । अनाद्यनन्तं महतः परं ध्रुवं निचाय्य तं मृत्युमुखात्प्रमुच्यते ’ (क. उ. १ । ३ । १५) इति । अत्र हि यादृशं शब्दादिहीनं प्रधानं महतः परं स्मृतौ निरूपितम् , तादृशमेव निचाय्यत्वेन निर्दिष्टम् । तस्मात्प्रधानमेवेदम् । तदेव चाव्यक्तशब्दनिर्दिष्टमिति । अत्र ब्रूमः — नेह प्रधानं निचाय्यत्वेन निर्दिष्टम् । प्राज्ञो हीह परमात्मा निचाय्यत्वेन निर्दिष्ट इति गम्यते । कुतः ? प्रकरणात् । प्राज्ञस्य हि प्रकरणं विततं वर्तते — ‘ पुरुषान्न परं किञ्चित्सा काष्ठा सा परा गतिः ’ इत्यादिनिर्देशात् , ‘ एष सर्वेषु भूतेषु गूढोऽऽत्मा न प्रकाशते ’ इति च दुर्ज्ञानत्ववचनेन तस्यैव ज्ञेयत्वाकाङ्क्षणात् , ‘ यच्छोद्वाङ्मनसी प्राज्ञः ’ इति च तज्ज्ञानायैव वागादिसंयमस्य विहितत्वात् । मृत्युमुखप्रमोक्षणफलत्वाच्च । न हि प्रधानमात्रं निचाय्य मृत्युमुखात्प्रमुच्यत इति साङ्ख्यैरिष्यते । चेतनात्मविज्ञानाद्धि मृत्युमुखात्प्रमुच्यत इति तेषामभ्युपगमः । सर्वेषु वेदान्तेषु प्राज्ञस्यैवात्मनोऽशब्दादिधर्मत्वमभिलप्यते । तस्मान्न प्रधानस्यात्र ज्ञेयत्वमव्यक्तशब्दनिर्दिष्टत्वं वा ॥ ५ ॥

## Translation

### Literal
Here the Sankhya says: the prior argument is unproved, because there is no statement that the referent is to be known. Why? Because later a statement of knowability is heard for what is denoted by the word avyakta, namely pradhana: 'that which is soundless, touchless, formless, undecaying, tasteless, eternal, scentless; beginningless, endless, higher than mahat, firm - having known that, one is released from the mouth of death' (Katha Up. 1.3.15). There, what is indicated as to-be-known matches exactly the pradhana described in Smriti as devoid of sound and the like and beyond mahat. Therefore this must be pradhana; and that indeed is what is denoted by avyakta. To this we reply: pradhana is not indicated here as what is to be known. Rather, it is the Pragna, the Supreme Self, that is indicated as to-be-known. Why? From the context. The context extends around Pragna: from declarations such as 'there is nothing higher than Purusha; that is the limit, that the highest goal'; from the statement of its difficulty of knowledge, 'this self hidden in all beings does not shine forth,' which creates the need to know that very reality; and from the injunction of speech-and-mind restraint precisely for the knowledge of that. Also because release from the mouth of death is stated as the fruit. Even Sankhyas do not hold that by knowing mere pradhana one is freed from death; they admit that liberation from death comes from knowledge of the conscious self. In all Vedanta texts, it is this Pragna-Self alone to which properties such as soundlessness are ascribed. Therefore here there is neither knowability of pradhana nor designation by the word avyakta. (5)

### Technical
Purvapaksha: Sankhya challenges the anti-pradhana reading by appealing to Katha Upanisad 1.3.15, arguing that the avyakta described as asabda, asparsa, arupa, and as 'beyond mahat' is explicitly presented as nicayya (to be known), and thus should be identified with pradhana. Siddhanta response: the referent is not pradhana but Pragna/Paramatman, established by prakarana-samarthya. The surrounding thread consistently targets the supreme self through: (1) explicit hierarchy culminating in Purusha; (2) avowed hiddenness that motivates inquiry; (3) prescribed discipline of speech-mind restraint for realization; and (4) promised fruit of release from death. Further, even Sankhya doctrine does not grant moksa from mere knowledge of inert pradhana; liberation is tied to awareness of conscious self. Therefore avyakta and knowability in this locus cannot be assigned to pradhana.

### Interpretive Note
Core move: semantic resemblance to Sankhya categories is overridden by full prakarana and soteriological consequence.
