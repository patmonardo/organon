## Translation

### Literal
Just as the term mahat, though used by Sankhyas for the first evolute, does not in Vedic usage necessarily denote that same entity - as shown by passages such as 'beyond intellect is the great Self,' 'having known the great all-pervading Self,' and 'I know that great Purusha,' where self-language governs the sense - so too the term avyakta in Vedic usage need not denote pradhana. Therefore the inferred Sankhya entity does not possess scriptural word-support. (7-bhashya)

### Technical
Argument by lexical non-isomorphism: technical terms in one system (e.g., Sankhya mahat) do not carry automatic denotation into shruti contexts. Vedic co-texts with atma/purusha markers resemanticize 'mahat' toward conscious self-reference. By parity, 'avyakta' cannot be fixed as pradhana merely through Sankhya convention. Conclusion: the anumaanika pradhana lacks independent sabda-pramana in this locus.

### Interpretive Note
Hermeneutic principle: shared vocabulary does not entail shared ontology.
