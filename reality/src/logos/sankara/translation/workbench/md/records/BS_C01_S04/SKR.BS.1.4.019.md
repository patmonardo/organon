## Translation

### Literal
Again, the proponent of pradhana argues that the claim 'pradhana has no scriptural word' is not established, because of mantra evidence: 'one unborn (female), red-white-black, producing many offspring of similar form; one unborn male enjoys her, another unborn leaves her after enjoyment' (Svet. Up. 4.5). Here, red-white-black are taken to denote rajas, sattva, and tamas: red by coloring-function, white by luminosity, black by covering-function. Their equilibrium state is described through these constituent-properties; and as unborn, this is called aja, consistent with accepting root prakrti as unmodified. Though aja is conventionally used for a she-goat, that conventional sense is inapplicable here because the context is knowledge-teaching. This aja generates many three-guna progeny. One purusha, delighting in that prakrti, lies with her and, through ignorance, identifying with her, wanders in samsara as happy, miserable, deluded. Another purusha, having arisen discriminative knowledge and detachment, abandons that prakrti after experience and is released. Therefore, the Kapila doctrine of pradhana and related categories is itself rooted in sruti - thus runs the prima facie conclusion.

### Technical
Purvapaksha reopens scriptural legitimacy for Sankhya via Svetasvatara 4.5. The mantra is read as encoding triguna-prakrti (aja) plus dual purusha modalities (bound enjoyer and liberated discriminator). Semantic mapping: lohita/sukla/krsna -> rajas/sattva/tamas; aja -> unproduced moola-prakrti; bhoga and tyaga narrative -> bondage and kaivalya trajectories. On this reading, Sankhya ontology is not merely inferential but sruti-supported, directly contesting the prior claim that pradhana lacks scriptural naming.

### Interpretive Note
Canonical Sankhya-style re-reading of the aja mantra is staged as a serious hermeneutic challenge.
