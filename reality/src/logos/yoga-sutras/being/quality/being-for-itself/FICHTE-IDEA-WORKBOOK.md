## The Bhasya: The Principle of True Infinity

Sūtra I.43 (_nirvitarkā_) documents the profound recovery of cognitive singularity. The mind purifies itself of discursive memory (_smṛti-pariśuddhau_), empties itself of its finite subjective form (_svarūpa-śūnyeva_), and shines entirely as the object alone (_artha-mātra-nirbhāsā_). Johann Gottlieb Fichte’s _Science of Knowing_ provides the deeper philosophical _Bhāṣya_ for this structural triumph: the attainment of True Infinity.

In Sūtra I.42 (_savitarkā_), the philosopher was trapped by the Anstoß (the check). The infinite outward activity of Knowing encountered a boundary, forcing it to retreat and exist as a finite, deliberative subject arguing against an external, finite object. This was a state of hostile dualism.

But pure philosophical science cannot rest in dualism. The philosopher realizes that the boundary (the object) is not an alien "thing-in-itself" standing arbitrarily outside the mind. It is actually a projection of the mind's own activity. Knowing _posits_ the boundary in order to reflect upon itself. When the philosopher grasps this truth, memory is purified: they stop reacting to objects as dead, historical facts dictated by language.

Because the object is recognized as the activity of Knowing itself, the "I" drops its defensive posture. The subjective observer empty its isolating form (_svarūpa-śūnyeva_). The opposition between knower and known collapses entirely, but not back into the empty, void crystal of I.41. It collapses into a massive, affirmative singularity: the living Light shining solely as its own content (_artha-mātra-nirbhāsā_).

This is what Fichte calls True Infinity and Absolute Self-Reference. The Light relates only to itself. It has sublated finite existence and achieved the absolute freedom of being completely present in its own act. This is the structural principle of Being-for-itself.
