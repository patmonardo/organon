## The Varttika: The Deep Demonstration of the One and Repulsion

While Fichte supplies the governing philosophical principle (True Infinity and the recovery of Absolute Light), Hegel supplies the _Varttika_—the microscopic transcendental proof of _how_ this recovery is logically enacted, and how it violently shatters.

Patañjali describes the ultimate triumph of the Quality triad as _nirvitarkā_ (the non-deliberative state). By purifying its memory of linguistic definitions, the mind drops its independent subjective status (_svarūpa-śūnyeva_) and becomes perfectly identified with the object (_artha-mātra-nirbhāsā_).

Hegel's _Science of Logic_ (from the extracted text in `prajna-greater-bhasya.tex`) models this exact architecture as **Being-for-itself**. In finite Existence (I.42), the mind was trapped by negative boundaries. To escape, the mind must negate that negation. It must push through the boundary. When it does, "negation is simple self-reference... absolutely determinate being."

Because it no longer references anything outside itself, Hegel defines this state as "the existent-for-itself, **the one**." This is the highest logical peak of Quality: a massive, un-mediated, non-deliberative singularity. The yogi has achieved the perfect "One."

But here the _Varttika_ unleashes its critical dialectical power. Patañjali presents _nirvitarkā_ as a supreme, stable spiritual achievement. Hegel demonstrates that the logical structure of "the One" is inherently explosive. Because "the One" is defined strictly by its absolute exclusion of any "other," it is essentially an engine of pure repulsion.

Hegel writes: "Second, the one passes over into a multiplicity of ones, repulsion." The singular, intense focus of the yogic mind cannot hold its absolute isolation. By aggressively repelling the "other," the One mathematically shatters itself into a plurality of identical Ones (the many). This immediate repulsion "sublates itself into its ideality, attraction... [where] the two sink into a state of equilibrium."

As the shattered Ones attract back together, they lose their distinct qualitative uniqueness and become a continuous, indifferent mass. Thus, Hegel concludes: "quality, driven to a head in being-for-itself, passes over into quantity." The brilliant, qualitative singularity of Sūtra I.43 inevitably triggers the descent into Sūtra I.44, where the mind will be forced to confront the subtle, sprawling, indifferent continuums of space and time.
