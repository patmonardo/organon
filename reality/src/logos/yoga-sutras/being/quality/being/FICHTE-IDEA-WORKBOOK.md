## The Bhasya: The Principle of the Absolute Light

Sūtra I.41 states that when fluctuations dwindle, the mind becomes like a transparent crystal, completely absorbing the knower, knowing, and known into a single unity (_samāpatti_). Johann Gottlieb Fichte’s _Science of Knowing_ (1804) acts as the _Bhāṣya_—the core, vital philosophical principle—animating this ancient text.

For Fichte, the "crystal" is the **Absolute Light**. To reach this Light, the philosopher must perform an act of absolute methodological suspension. Everyday consciousness is scattered, constantly erecting factical boundaries and assuming that objects exist entirely independent of the mind. Fichte demands that we suspend this agitated projection (the exact equivalent of _kṣīṇa-vṛtti_, the dwindling of fluctuations).

When this suspension is achieved, what remains is not an empty, dead void. What remains is the absolute principle of Knowing itself: pure, unadulterated Light. This Light is defined by its immaculate self-transparency. It does not look outward to find an object to illuminate; its very nature is to illuminate itself.

In this state of pure immanence, the devastating philosophical dualism that plagues ordinary thought—the gap between the Subject (the "I") and the Object (the "Not-I")—is utterly overcome. The _grahītṛ_ (cognizer), _grahaṇa_ (cognition), and _grāhyeṣu_ (cognized) are revealed as a single, unbroken continuum of Light. Fichte declares this as the absolute starting point of all true philosophical science. Before the hiatus irrationalis rips reality into a subject confronting an alien object, there is the immediate, luminous identity of the Absolute. This is the philosophical principle of Pure Being.
