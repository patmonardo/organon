## Narrative Synthesis: The Transparent Crystal

Sūtra I.41 (_kṣīṇa-vṛtter abhijātasyeva maṇer..._) establishes the foundational baseline of the mind. It is the absolute starting point of profound yogic epistemology. Before analyzing the complex ways the mind might err, construct reality, or eventually achieve liberation, Patañjali defines what the mind is when its agitated fluctuations (_vṛtti_) have finally ceased (_kṣīṇa_).

The metaphor provided is profound: the mind is _abhijātasyeva maṇer_—like a pristine, flawlessly transparent crystal. What characterizes a transparent crystal? It has no color of its own. It is defined precisely by its emptiness, its lack of inherent determination. It is pure receptivity. When an object is placed near the crystal, the crystal immediately takes on the color of that object (_tat-stha-tad-añjanatā_).

This state is called _Samāpatti_ (total cognitive absorption). In this pristine condition, the rigorous, rigid boundaries that normally separate the Knower (_grahītṛ_), the Act of knowing (_grahaṇa_), and the Object known (_grāhyeṣu_) are smoothed out into an unbroken unity. The mind does not struggle to know the object; it simply becomes the object. The subject does not stand apart from the act; the subject, act, and object are unified in the singular, transparent flash of the crystal's coloration.

Thus, Sūtra I.41 defines the domain of Pure Being for the Yoga Sūtras. It is the sphere of absolute immediacy. There is no mediation, no deliberation, no questioning of the object's reality—there is only the pure, indeterminate transparency of the crystal, resting identically within itself and whatever object it simultaneously assumes. It is the perfect, motionless stage upon which all subsequent dialectical and cognitive movements will play out.
