## Narrative Synthesis: The Contamination of the Sign

Following the profound silence of the transparent crystal in Sūtra I.41, Sūtra I.42 documents the mind's catastrophic fall into discursive complexity: _tatra śabda-artha-jñāna-vikalpaiḥ saṃkīrṇā savitarkā samāpattiḥ_.

What causes the mind to fall from pure, immediate Being into a restricted, deliberative mode of existence? Patañjali identifies the culprit precisely: the linguistic mechanism of the sign. The pristine cognitive absorption is _saṃkīrṇā_—mixed, blended, or contaminated. It is polluted by _vikalpa_ (conceptual construction).

Instead of perfectly mirroring the object in an unbroken unity, the mind suddenly carves the event of knowing into three separate, artificial pillars:

1. **Śabda**: The word, the name, the linguistic signifier.
2. **Artha**: The meaning, the intended object itself.
3. **Jñāna**: The knowledge, the subjective mental representation.

Because the mind conflates the word for the object with the actual object, it loses immediate contact with reality. It traps itself in a hall of mirrors, endlessly deliberating, parsing, and defining. This state of cognitive absorption is called _savitarkā_ (deliberative or argumentative).

This is no longer Pure Being. The mind now _exists_ as a specific, finite, conceptualizing entity, constantly opposing its linguistic definitions against the fluid reality of the object. The subject and object are ripped apart, mediated entirely by the "word."
