## The Bhasya: The Principle of the Abstract Limit

Sūtra I.45 establishes that the mind's journey through the sprawling subtle continuum (_sūkṣma-viṣayā_) is not endless. It hits a definitive wall: the _aliṅga_ (the unmanifest, unmarked terminus). Johann Gottlieb Fichte’s _Science of Knowing_ provides the deeper philosophical _Bhāṣya_ for why this wall exists: the Living Light must logically create an abstract limit (Quantum/Degree) to measure its own infinite activity.

In the previous stage (YS I.44), the philosopher unleashed the Continuous Productive Imagination. The rigid boundaries of objects were gone, and Knowing swept outward as pure, open extension. But Fichte identifies a severe danger here: pure, boundless continuity is practically indistinguishable from Nothingness. If the mind extends infinitely without a marker, it loses consciousness of itself. Knowing requires a reference point.

Therefore, the active "I" must perform an act of sheer will. It must arbitrarily arrest its own continuity. It says: "Thus far, and no further." This arrest is the generation of the limit (_paryavasānam_).

Because the gross object was already destroyed, this limit cannot be a physical "thing." It is simply an abstract line drawn in the darkness. It has no color, no shape, no "mark" or characteristic. It is perfectly _aliṅga_ (unmarked). This is Fichte’s definition of mathematical "Degree." The philosopher actively forces the quantitative continuum to become bounded. Knowing bounds itself, generating the Quantum. By doing so, the mind anchors itself, preventing it from evaporating endlessly into abstract space. This proves that the unmanifest ground (_aliṅga_) is not an ancient physical substance lurking in the universe, but the active, abstract limit posited by the Intelligence itself.
