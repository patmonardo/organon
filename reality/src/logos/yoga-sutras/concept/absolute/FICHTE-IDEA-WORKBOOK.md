## The Closure of the Science of Knowing: Reason Making Itself

The Yoga Sūtras mapping for III.53-56 aligns perfectly with the momentous transition in Fichte's 1804 _Science of Knowing_, specifically Lectures 26 through 28 (the beginning of the post-prolegomena week). In these lectures, Fichte moves from establishing formal certainty to demonstrating how absolute reason constitutes itself, culminating in the complete closure of the transcendental enterprise. This perfectly mirrors Patañjali’s trajectory from temporal discrimination to the final isolation of _Kaivalya_.

### Formal Certainty and LogoGenesis (Mapping YS III.53)

Patañjali’s focus on the atom of time (_kṣaṇa_) and sequence (_krama_) as the genesis of discriminative knowledge finds its reflection in Fichte’s Lecture 26. For Fichte, knowing cannot simply pre-assume "the Concept" as a static premise. The truth must be achieved via _LogoGenesis_—the active, genetic derivation of the Concept through formal certainty.

Fichte asserts, "We are absolute knowing only if we are formally certain of it and express this certainty in fulfilling the knowing." Discrimination of the temporal sequence (_krama_) is analogous to the "creative intuition" Fichte commends to his listeners. The "We" (the philosophers/scientists of knowing) is constituted precisely by the pure ideal seeing that tracks this genesis. By seeing how the Concept forms itself out of the immediate projection of the described, one breaks the illusion of unexamined givenness, achieving the formal certainty required to proceed.

### Synthesis of Objectivity and Genesis (Mapping YS III.54)

Yoga’s criterion of sameness—when class, mark, and place (_jāti_, _lakṣaṇa_, _deśa_) fail to delimit, identity is recognized—corresponds to Fichte’s profound realization in late Lecture 26 regarding externalization and the "drive."

Fichte seeks a primordial description of certainty as "immanent self-enclosure." The illusion of ordinary reality is caused by a metaphysical "drive" to externalize, which posits differences, multiplicities, and delimitations. However, when we apply the pure ideal seeing, this projective drive is forced to "self-recognize and negate itself." When the markers of externalization are subtracted, what remains is the pure synthesis: objectivity and genesis are entirely one. Being is discovered only residing within absolute seeing itself; there is no hidden "in-itself" lurking behind the markers.

### The Absolute Insight of Reason (Mapping YS III.55)

The overwhelming magnitude of _Tāraka_ in YS III.55—knowing all objects in all respects without sequence (_akramam_)—is the exact equivalent of Fichte’s "Absolute Insight of Reason" described in Lecture 27.

Fichte argues that once seeing posits itself as an absolute act, it immediately bridges essence and existence. The "seeing posited as seeing necessarily occurs." In this space, seeing is "powerful, living, active." It is unmediated (_akrama_). The Absolute Insight of Reason is entirely self-determining and self-positing. Because it constitutes the very ground of existence through its own self-expression, it cannot possibly leave any object or "respect" outside of its domain (_sarva-viṣaya_, _sarvathā-viṣaya_). Fichte establishes here his Doctrine of Reason: reason speaking directly to itself about itself, constituting the highest and most comprehensive part of the Science of Knowing.

### The Removal of Ambiguity and Closure (Mapping YS III.56)

Patañjali defines _Kaivalya_ as the equality of purity between the instrumental mirror (_sattva_) and the pure witness (_Puruṣa_). In Lecture 28, Fichte provides the exact philosophical mechanism for this absolute liberation via the resolution of a foundational ambiguity.

Fichte observes that Reason seems to suffer a dual occurrence: it appears as Subject (in us, the observers) and as Object (the outward absolute Reason). Which one is primary? Fichte demands that we step into the exact "midpoint," recognizing that "permanent object and permanent subject arise from the same original essential effect." The "making of being" and the "making of making" stem from a single, unbroken source.

When the philosopher refrains from objectifying this midpoint—when the intellect/instrument mirrors this living source with zero distortion (_śuddhi-sāmya_)—Subject and Object fall into absolute equality. The gap closes. If one fails to hold this midpoint, the system splinters into "fivefoldness" or a multiplication of principles (sensibility, legality, morality, religion), much like the 25 _tattvas_ of Sāṃkhya falling into manifestation. But by maintaining this equality of purity, the Science is finished. Ambiguity is resolved. Freedom and Independence (_Kaivalya_) are achieved, and Fichte triumphantly declares: "The task is finished; the science has closed."
