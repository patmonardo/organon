# YS III.17-20: Fichte Mapping - Cognition and Theoretical Analysis

## 1. The Principle of Transcendental Distinction

In Fichte's system, everyday consciousness is trapped in a dogmatic slumber because it conflates the subject with the object. It believes the object "causes" the thought. The entire _Science of Knowing_ is an exercise in rigorous, painful separation—learning to distinguish the pure act of positing from the heavy, dead feeling of the "given" object.

Patañjali’s YS III.17 is the exact methodological equivalent of Fichte’s philosophical starting point.

### 1.1 Tearing Apart the Superimposition

Patañjali states that word, meaning, and idea are superimposed (_adhyāsa_) and confused (_saṅkara_). This is the state of empirical consciousness. To the ordinary person, the word "dog", the physical dog, and the idea of the dog are a single tangled knot.

Fichte argues that to achieve freedom, the "I" must forcefully separate these elements. Patañjali prescribes _Samyama_ upon their distinction (_pravibhāga_). By separating the pure subjective act (the idea) from the objective reference (the meaning) and the mechanical medium (the word), the ego realizes that it is the author of the synthesis. Having cracked the synthesis, the ego can understand any language, because all language is just variations on this subjective-objective synthesis.

### 1.2 Objectifying the Subject

In Sutras III.18 and III.19, the Yogin applies this penetrating theoretical gaze to the internal world. By bringing to light the hidden subconscious impressions (_saṃskāra_) and the cognitions of others (_para-citta_), the Yogin is demonstrating the absolute reach of the Theoretical "I".

Nothing is hidden from the absolute "I". The past history of the ego (past lives) is dragged into the immediate present as an object of knowledge. The private inner worlds of the non-I (other minds) are penetrated and rendered transparent. The ego proves that the entire universe, both physical and psychological, is completely permeated by the light of theoretical reason.
