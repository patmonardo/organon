# YS III.17-20: Hegel Mapping - Cognition

## 1. Hegel's Doctrine of the Concept: The Idea -> Cognition

In Hegel's _Science of Logic_, the immediate unity of **Life** must divide itself in order to become self-conscious. Life exists, but it does not _know_ that it exists until it splits into Subject and Object. This split, and the drive to overcome it through knowledge, is the **Idea of Cognition**.

**YS III.17-19:** The Yogin applies Samyama to separate the word, meaning, and idea; to directly recognize subconscious impressions; and to cognize other minds.

### 1.1 The Presupposition of the Objective World

Hegel writes: "The concept has first liberated itself into itself, giving itself only a still abstract objectivity for its reality... finite, that is, subjective spirit, makes for itself the presupposition of an objective world... but its activity is the sublating of this presupposition and the turning of it into something posited."

In YS III.17, the "presupposition" is the confused mixture (_saṅkara_) of word, object, and idea. Naive consciousness takes this fusion as a given objective fact. The activity of Cognition is the tearing apart (_pravibhāga_) of this fusion. The Yogin sublates the presupposition by breaking the communication down into its conceptual components, thereby making it "something posited" by the subject. Through this, the Yogin achieves universal translation (knowledge of all sounds).

### 1.2 The Theoretical Idea

Hegel calls this immediate form of cognition the "theoretical idea." The subjective concept refers to an external world "in the absolute certainty of itself, in order to elevate its implicit reality, this formal truth, to real truth."

Sutras III.18 and III.19 are pure acts of the Theoretical Idea. The Yogin projects theoretical certainty onto the most hidden aspects of reality: the deep subconscious impressions (_saṃskāras_) and the private minds of others (_para-citta_). The Yogin reads the objective world (even when that world is another's subjectivity) and converts it perfectly into its own internal concept.

### 1.3 The Limit of Finite Cognition (YS III.20)

Hegel notes that in finite cognition, the objective world retains a distinctness; the true and the good are still separated. Patañjali perfectly delineates this finite boundary in YS III.20: the Yogin reads the other's mind, but does not take on the _object_ (_sālambanaṃ_) of that mind. The cognition remains theoretical; it knows the form but does not practically absorb the content. The subject remains safely distinct from the object it observes.
