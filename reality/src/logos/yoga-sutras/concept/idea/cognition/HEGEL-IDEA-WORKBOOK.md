# Concept/Idea/Cognition - HEGEL IDEA WORKBOOK

## The Demonstration (_Varttika_): The Tearing Apart of the Immediate Idea

Fichte outlines the ego's necessary analytic penetration of the "given." Hegel’s _Science of Logic_ provides the transcendental demonstration of how the immediate, blissful unity of **Life** logically _must_ fracture itself, passing over into the Theoretical Idea, or **Cognition**.

**1. The Judgment of Life**
In Life (YS III.16), the subject and the objective timeline were immediately unified. But Hegel writes that this immediate unity is "still internally unrealized." For Life to truly _know_ itself, it must step back from itself. "In its judgment, the idea is cognition in general."

This "judgment" is the literal translation of _pravibhāga_ (distinction) in YS III.17. The Yogin recognizes that the immediate experience of the world is a confused mixture (_saṅkara_). To achieve true Cognition, the Theoretical Idea must tear this mixture apart. The Yogin asserts the Concept by separating the objective word from the subjective idea. This analytic action proves that the knowing subject is not just blindly living; it is universally determining the structure of reality.

**2. The Theoretical Sublimation of the World**
Hegel describes the activity of finite cognition as making "the presupposition of an objective world... but its activity is the sublating of this presupposition and the turning of it into something posited."

YS III.18 and III.19 are masterful examples of this sublating activity. The Yogin faces the ultimate objective barriers to knowledge: the deep, inaccessible past of the subconscious (_saṃskāras_), and the opaque, sealed minds of others (_para-citta_). Through _Samyama_, the Yogin sublates these barriers. The objective, hidden world is turned into "something posited" within the Yogin's own certainty. Subjective Spirit realizes that the objective world "is the ideality in which it knows itself." It looks into the mind of the other, and sees its own conceptual structure.

**3. The Finite Limit**
But Hegel notes that cognition, at this theoretical stage, is still finite. The subject and object are unified in thought, but they are still distinct. Patañjali captures this precise logical boundary in YS III.20: the Yogin knows the other mind, but not its supporting object (_na ca tat sālambanaṃ_). The Theoretical Idea is perfect in its grasping of the _form_ of the other mind, but it stops short of fully merging with its material _content_. The final unity of Cognition and Will has not yet been achieved; that awaits the Absolute Idea.
