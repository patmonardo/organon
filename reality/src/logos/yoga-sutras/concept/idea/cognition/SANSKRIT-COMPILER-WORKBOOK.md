# YS III.17-20: The Analytic Power of Cognition

## 1. Sanskrit Text & Transliteration

**Sutra III.17**
शब्दार्थप्रत्ययानामितरेतराध्यासात्सङ्करस्तत्प्रविभागसंयमात्सर्वभूतरुतज्ञानम्॥ ३.१७॥
_śabdārtha-pratyayānām itaretarādhyāsāt saṅkaras tat-pravibhāga-saṃyamāt sarva-bhūta-ruta-jñānam_

**Sutra III.18**
संस्कारसाक्षात्करणात्पूर्वजातिज्ञानम्॥ ३.१८॥
_saṃskāra-sākṣātkaraṇāt pūrva-jāti-jñānam_

**Sutra III.19**
प्रत्ययस्य परचित्तज्ञानम्॥ ३.१९॥
_pratyayasya para-citta-jñānam_

**Sutra III.20**
न च तत्सालम्बनं तस्याविषयीभूतत्वात्॥ ३.२०॥
_na ca tat sālambanaṃ tasyāviṣayībhūtatvāt_

## 2. Philological Breakdown

- **śabda-artha-pratyayānām:** of word, meaning/object, and idea/cognition.
- **adhyāsāt:** due to superimposition.
- **saṅkarah:** confusion; mixture; blending.
- **pravibhāga:** distinction; separation; analysis.
- **saṃskāra:** subconscious impressions.
- **sākṣāt-karaṇāt:** from direct realization/bringing before the eyes.
- **para-citta-jñānam:** knowledge of another's mind.
- **sālambanaṃ:** with its support/object.

## 3. Direct Translation

**III.17:** "The word, the meaning, and the idea are confused due to superimposition upon one another. By applying Samyama on their distinction (_pravibhāga_), there arises knowledge of the sounds (languages) of all beings."
**III.18:** "By direct realization of subconscious impressions (_saṃskāra_), there arises knowledge of previous births."
**III.19:** "By direct realization of [another's] idea/cognition, there arises knowledge of another's mind."
**III.20:** "But not with its supporting object, because that is not the subject of the observation."

## 4. Systemic Analysis

Following the immediate realization of Life (YS III.16), the Idea must fracture into self-conscious **Cognition**. The immediate unity must be analytically divided by the subject to achieve true theoretical knowledge.

### The Problem of Superimposition (_Adhyāsa_)

In ordinary consciousness, the word spoken, the physical object it refers to, and the mental idea of it are fused tightly together in a confused mixture (_saṅkarah_). This is the "given" objective world. The ego naively accepts this fused package.

### The Power of Distinction (_Pravibhāga_)

Cognition begins when the Yogin applies _Samyama_ not to fuse things together, but to tear them apart (_pravibhāga_). By separating the signifier from the signified and from the subjective concept, the Yogin cracks the code of all language. Theoretical cognition is the act of sublating the naive "givenness" of the world and recognizing the distinct conceptual architecture behind it.

### The Objectification of the Subjective

This analytical cognition extends directly into psychology. By directly observing the hidden structural layers of the mind—the subconscious impressions (_saṃskāra_) and the bare flow of cognition (_pratyaya_)—the Yogin gains theoretical knowledge of past lives and other minds. The Yogin is reading the "code" of subjectivity itself as if it were an objective text, yet carefully maintaining the boundary (Sutra 20 points out that while the Yogin reads the other mind, the Yogin does not get entangled in the _objects_ that other mind is perceiving).
