# YS III.21-22: Hegel Mapping - Speculation (Absolute Idea)

## 1. Hegel's Doctrine of the Concept: The Idea -> Absolute Idea

In Hegel's _Science of Logic_, Cognition is still finite because it leaves an unresolved tension between the subject's theoretical desire to know the world and its practical will to alter it. The dialectic completes itself only in the **Absolute Idea** (Speculation), where Theoretical and Practical Reason perfectly coincide.

**YS III.21-22:** "By applying Samyama on the form of the body... there is invisibility."

### 1.1 The Identity of Theoretical and Practical

Hegel writes: "The absolute idea has shown itself to be the identity of the theoretical and the practical idea... As the rational concept that in its reality only rejoins itself... it harbors the most extreme opposition within."

Invisibility (_antardhānam_) is the perfect demonstration of this identity. Theoretically, the Yogin perfectly understands the physical mechanism of light, sight, and form (_kāya-rūpa_, _cakṣuḥ-prakāśa_). Practically, the Yogin exercises absolute sovereignty over it, suspending its power (_śakti-stambhe_). The Yogin does not just "know" the physics; the Yogin _is_ the physics, and therefore can halt its operation. The theoretical understanding of the object is simultaneously the practical mastery of the object.

### 1.2 The Impenetrability of the Pure Concept

Hegel describes the Absolute Idea as "the pure concept that has itself as its subject matter... as person impenetrable, atomic subjectivity."

When the Yogin becomes invisible, they are literally demonstrating "impenetrable subjectivity." They cease to be an object (_rūpa_) for any other finite consciousness. The Yogin withdraws into the pure rationality of the Concept. "All the rest is error, confusion... the absolute idea alone is being, imperishable life." The Yogin, in severing the connection of light (_asamprayoge_), proves that their existence is not dependent on empirical manifestation. They exist absolutely, in and for themselves, immune to the gaze of the "Other."
