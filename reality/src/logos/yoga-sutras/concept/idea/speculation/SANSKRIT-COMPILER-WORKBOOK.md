# YS III.21-22: The Absolute Withholding (Speculation)

## 1. Sanskrit Text & Transliteration

**Sutra III.21**
कायरूपसंयमात्तद्ग्राह्यशक्तिस्तम्भे चक्षुःप्रकाशासम्प्रयोगेऽन्तर्धानम्॥ ३.२१॥
_kāya-rūpa-saṃyamāt tad-grāhya-śakti-stambhe cakṣuḥ-prakāśāsamprayoge 'ntardhānam_

**Sutra III.22**
एतेन शब्दाद्यन्तर्धानमुक्तम्॥ ३.२२॥
_etena śabdādyantardhānam uktam_

## 2. Philological Breakdown

- **kāya:** body.
- **rūpa:** form; visual appearance.
- **saṃyamāt:** from applying Samyama.
- **tad-grāhya:** its perceptibility; the capacity to be grasped/seen.
- **śakti:** power; capacity.
- **stambhe:** upon suspension; blockage; stoppage.
- **cakṣuḥ:** eye.
- **prakāśa:** light.
- **asamprayoge:** upon the disconnection; non-contact.
- **antardhānam:** invisibility; disappearance.
- **etena:** by this.
- **śabda-ādi:** sound and so forth (the other sense objects).
- **uktam:** is explained; is said.

## 3. Direct Translation

**III.21:** "By applying Samyama on the form of the body, upon the suspension of its receptive power, and upon the disconnection of the light of the eye, there is invisibility (_antardhānam_)."
**III.22:** "By this, the disappearance of sound and the rest is explained."

## 4. Systemic Analysis

After the analytic tearing apart of reality in Cognition (YS III.17-20), the Idea completes its logical circuit. The Yogin does not just observe the world theoretically; the Yogin exercises absolute practical sovereignty over it. This is Speculation, or the Absolute Idea.

### The Absolute Identity of Theoretical and Practical

In Cognition, the subject and object were still slightly separated (the Yogin read the other mind but did not merge with its object). In _antardhānam_ (invisibility), this limit is shattered.

The Yogin applies _Samyama_ to the very interface between the subject and the objective world—specifically, the mechanism of light hitting the eye (_cakṣuḥ-prakāśa_). By intellectually grasping the rule of perception (_tad-grāhya-śakti_), the Yogin practically suspends it (_stambhe_). The subjective knowledge of physics immediately translates into absolute practical control over physics. The body vanishes.

### The Retreat into the Pure Concept

Invisibility is the perfect metaphor for the Absolute Idea. By becoming invisible, the Yogin withdraws completely from the objective, empirical realm of phenomenal appearance (_rūpa_). The Yogin proves that they are not bound to be an object for another subject. The "I" becomes pure, impenetrable Subjectivity—the Concept resting entirely within itself. God-consciousness. The Yogin is present, but perfectly unmanifest, just as the Absolute Idea contains the totality of reality within itself without needing to cast a shadow in finite space.
