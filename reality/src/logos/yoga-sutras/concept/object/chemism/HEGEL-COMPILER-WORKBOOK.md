# YS III.14: Hegel Mapping - Chemism

## 1. Hegel's Doctrine of the Concept: Objectivity -> Chemism

In Hegel's _Science of Logic_, Mechanism (the mapping for YS III.13) posits a world of objects that are indifferent to one another. Their interactions are purely external. However, the internal necessity of the Concept cannot rest in mere externality. It must reveal that these seemingly indifferent objects are fundamentally related through an underlying affinity. This brings us to **Chemism**.

**YS III.14:** "The substratum (_dharmī_) is that to which the subsided, arisen, and unnamable characteristics (_dharma_) adhere."

### 1.1 From Indifference to Affinity

Hegel writes: "Inasmuch as in mechanism that unity reveals itself to be the immanent law of the objects, their relation becomes one of non-indifference, each specifically different according to law; a connection in which the objects' determinate self-subsistence is sublated. This is chemism."

Mechanism viewed objects passing through time (_pariṇāma_) as simply being shoved along via external force. YS III.14 reveals the _dharmī_—the underlying substance that persists _through_ the changing characteristics (_dharma_). The forms (past, present, future) are no longer indifferent, isolated moments. They belong to a chemical affinity. They are bound together by the inner necessity of the substance.

### 1.2 The Chemical Object's Incompleteness

The chemical object (the _dharmī_) is in a state of tension. It is complete in itself as substance, yet it is relentlessly driven through the phases of time (_śānta, udita, avyapadeśya_). Hegel defines the chemical object as essentially characterized by "determinateness and positedness." It is driven into a process because it seeks to complete the totality of its Concept.

Patañjali’s _dharmī_ perfectly mirrors this. It is the single substance running through the three phases of time. It is internally divided by time, yet it provides the unbroken continuity that binds past, present, and future into a single, cohesive totality. The object is no longer dead mechanics; it has an inherent, substantial life.
