# Concept/Object/Chemism - HEGEL IDEA WORKBOOK

## The Demonstration (_Varttika_): The Sublation of Immediate Objectivity

Fichte identifies the _dharmī_ (substance) as the necessary proof that time is not a discontinuous void. Hegel’s _Science of Logic_ provides the precise demonstration of how Mechanism—the realm of indifferent, isolated objects—breaks down under its own logical weight and inevitably transitions into **Chemism**, the realm of objective affinity.

**1. The Breakdown of Mechanism**
In Mechanism (YS III.13), objects were complete totalities existing outside of one another. Their transformations (form, time, phase) were purely external. But Hegel demonstrates a fatal contradiction here: if they are truly self-subsistent and indifferent, they could not affect one another at all. They could not undergo _pariṇāma_ (transformation).

The very fact that they change implies an inner necessity, an "immanent law." The objects are not actually indifferent; they are biased toward their own unfolding. Their determinate self-subsistence must be sublated.

**2. The Affinity of the _Dharmī_**
This sublating of mechanical indifference is the birth of Chemism. The object is revealed to be a _dharmī_—a substance in tension. Patañjali says the _dharmī_ is that which "closely follows and is common to" (_anupātī_) its past, present, and latent future properties.

Hegel writes exactly this. The chemical object is characterized by "determinateness and positedness." It is not at rest. It is a substratum that is compelled to run through a process because it is inherently related to its own otherness (its past and its future). The _dharmas_ (properties) are the shifting phenomenal expressions of a substance trying to complete its own Concept.

**3. The Tension of Chemism**
Because the chemical object (the _dharmī_) is spread across time—holding the subsided (_śānta_), the manifest (_udita_), and the unnamable (_avyapadeśya_)—it is a living contradiction. It is one substance, but it is divided by time. It has an affinity for its own future states and is repelled by its passed states.

Therefore, Objectivity, at the stage of Chemism, is swept up in a relentless process. The object is furiously trying to bind its temporal parts together into a lasting, self-determined whole. But Chemism alone cannot achieve this final unity; it must transition into the self-determining purpose of Teleology (YS III.15) to finally close the loop.
