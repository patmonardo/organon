# YS III.13: Fichte Mapping - Mechanism and the Immanence of the Law

## 1. The Principle of Externalization

If the preceding three sutras (YS III.9-12) detailed the Fichtean "artificial intuition" where the ego inwardly deduces its own necessary structure, YS III.13 represents the turn towards the Objective.

Patañjali's pivot—stating that the transformations of the mind explain the transformations of the physical elements and sense organs—reflects Fichte's core epistemological deduction: **The laws of the objective world are nothing but the externalized laws of the Subject.**

### 1.1 The Transparency of the "Given"

In naive realism, the world of elements (_bhūtas_) and senses (_indriyas_) is assumed to be an alien mechanism operating independently of thought. Fichte's transcendental idealism systematically destroys this assumption.

The assertion (_etena... vyākhyātāḥ_) "by this is explained" demonstrates that the physical laws of nature (the _dharma, lakṣaṇa, avasthā_ transformations) are totally transparent to the ego. The ego generated them. The mechanism of nature is determined by the identical structural necessity that determines the Syllogism of the mind.

### 1.2 The Dead Letter of the Object

Fichte often speaks of the objective world, when stripped of its unifying Spirit, as a dead letter, a sheer mechanism. This corresponds to the objective world appearing as a sequence of blind transformations. However, for the transcendental philosopher (the accomplished Yogin), this mechanism is no longer terrifying or opaque. Because the Yogin has mastered the inward sequence of Subjectivity, the outward sequence of Objectivity is laid bare as a mere mirror—a mechanical reflection of the Concept.

The ego encounters the object not as an insurmountable other, but as a mechanism constructed by its own universal laws.
