# YS III.13: Hegel Mapping - Mechanism

## 1. Hegel's Doctrine of the Concept: Objectivity -> Mechanism

In Hegel's _Science of Logic_, once the Subjective Concept has completed itself in the true Syllogism (the corresponding mapping for YS III.12), it steps forth out of subjectivity into **Objectivity**. Objectivity is the totality of the Concept that has returned into its unity, but an immediacy is posited that is, at first, indifferent.

The first stage of Objectivity is **Mechanism**.

**YS III.13:** "By this [internal transformation], the transformations of form, time, and state in the elements and sense organs are explained."

### 1.1 The Transition to Immediate Objectivity

Hegel writes: "Since objectivity is the totality of the concept that has returned into its unity, an immediate is thereby posited which is in and for itself that totality... but in it the negativity of the concept has as yet not detached itself from the immediacy of the totality."

This aligns perfectly with _etena_ ("by this"). Patañjali declares that the objective world (_bhūtendriyeṣu_) is explained by the exact same structural logic as the subjective mind. The subjective concept has externalized itself.

### 1.2 The Indifference of the Object

In Mechanism, the objects stand in "self-subsistent indifference" as objects "each outside the other."
Patañjali’s threefold transformation of the objective world (_dharma, lakṣaṇa, avasthā_) describes the mechanical rules governing this indifferent objectivity. A clay pot changes form, moves through time, and ages. These are objective facts happening "out there."

Hegel writes: "This is what constitutes the character of mechanism, namely, that whatever the connection that obtains between the things combined, the connection remains one that is alien to them, that does not affect their nature..."

The objective world operates mechanically because the objects appear as isolated units merely undergoing external transformations. Yet, because the Yoga Sutras state that these external transformations are explained "by this" (the internal logic of _Samyama_), it reveals that mechanism is not ultimately alien to the mind; the mind's logic encompasses mechanical laws.
