# YS III.13: The Objective Translation of Samyama (Mechanism)

## 1. Sanskrit Text & Transliteration

**Sutra III.13**
एतेन भूतेन्द्रियेषु धर्मलक्षणावस्थापरिणामा व्याख्याताः ॥ ३.१३॥
_etena bhūtendriyeṣu dharma-lakṣaṇāvasthā-pariṇāmā vyākhyātāḥ_

## 2. Philological Breakdown

- **etena (एतेन):** by this; through this (referring to the preceding three subjective transformations).
- **bhūta (भूत):** elements; gross or subtle material elements.
- **indriyeṣu (इन्द्रियेषु):** in the sense organs; in the instruments of perception and action.
- **dharma (धर्म):** form; characteristic; property.
- **lakṣaṇa (लक्षण):** time-character; temporal phase (past, present, future).
- **avasthā (अवस्था):** condition; state; aging or intensity.
- **pariṇāmāḥ (परिणामाः):** (the) transformations; changes.
- **vyākhyātāḥ (व्याख्याताः):** are described; are explained.

## 3. Direct Translation

"By this [same threefold internal transformation], the transformations of form (dharma), time-character (lakṣaṇa), and condition (avasthā) in the elements (bhūtas) and sense organs (indriyas) have been explained."

## 4. Systemic Analysis

This sutra marks a pivotal transition in the Yoga Sutras. Having thoroughly mapped the internal, subjective transformations of the mind (_nirodha_, _samādhi_, _ekāgratā_), Patañjali declares that the exact same dialectical logic applies to the objective world.

### The Objective World (Bhūtas and Indriyas)

The "elements" (the objective raw material of reality) and the "sense organs" (the cognitive apparatus that interacts with reality) are not governed by alien laws. They are governed by the exact same principle of transformation (_pariṇāma_) that the subjective mind is.

### The Threefold Objective Transformation

1.  **Dharma-pariṇāma (Transformation of Form/Property):** The transition of a substance from one inherent property to another (e.g., clay becoming a pot). This is the immediate, qualitative change.
2.  **Lakṣaṇa-pariṇāma (Transformation of Time-Character):** The transition of an object through time: moving from the unmanifest future, into the active present, and subsiding into the latent past.
3.  **Avasthā-pariṇāma (Transformation of Condition):** The continuous deterioration or intensification of an object within the present moment (e.g., a new pot aging into an old pot).

By stating _etena_ ("by this"), the text asserts that the objective world operates mechanically, but that this mechanism is fully transparent to, and isomorphic with, the realized subjective Concept. The world is a machine of transformations, perfectly aligned with the logical structure of thought.
