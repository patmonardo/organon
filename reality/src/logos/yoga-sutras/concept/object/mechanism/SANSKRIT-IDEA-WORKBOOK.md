# Concept/Object/Mechanism - SANSKRIT IDEA WORKBOOK

## The Synthesis: The Architecture of the Objective Turn

Yoga Sutra III.13 is a hinge that swings the entirety of Patañjali's psychic mapping out toward the physical universe. After detailing the rigorous internal transformations of the mind (_Nirodha_, _Samādhi_, _Ekāgratā_), the text states: "By this, the transformations of form, time, and condition in the elements and sense organs are explained." This aligns perfectly with the transition from Subjectivity to Objectivity in German Idealism, specifically the stage of Mechanism.

We can synthesize these dialects as follows:

**1. The "Etena" (By This) as the Bridge to Objectivity**
In Hegel’s Logic, once the Subjective Concept completes itself in the Syllogism, it does not stop; it externalizes itself. Its internal perfection forces it to step forth as an immediate, objective world. Patañjali captures this precise dialectical leap with a single word: _Etena_ ("By this"). He posits an absolute isomorphism between the inner laws of meditation and the outer laws of physics. The physical elements (_bhūtas_) do not operate on a different operating system than the mind. They operate on the exact same threefold logic, just externalized.

**2. The Mechanics of Physical Transformation (_Pariṇāma_)**
Hegel defines the first stage of Objectivity as Mechanism. Here, objects exist as independent totalities that relate to each other externally. Any change they undergo is an alteration from the outside.

Patañjali describes this mechanical world as being governed by three specific external transformations:

- _Dharma-pariṇāma_: The change of qualitative form (e.g., dust becoming a clump of clay).
- _Lakṣaṇa-pariṇāma_: The movement along the timeline (past, present, future).
- _Avasthā-pariṇāma_: The intensive aging or conditioning of the state (e.g., the clay drying out).

These are perfectly mechanical descriptions. They track the "indifferent" physical bodies as they bump into one another across space and time.

**3. The Mastery of the Machine**
For Fichte, recognizing that the objective world is just the externalized mechanism of the Subject is the moment of absolute liberation. The "Thing-in-Itself" dies. The Yogin who reaches YS III.13 realizes that the crushing weight of physical reality (_prakṛti_) is actually just their own rational architecture looking back at them in the mirror of matter. Because the Yogin has mastered the cause (the internal logic of _Samyama_), the effect (the mechanical transformations of the elements) is no longer a mystery, but a tool ready to be wielded. This theoretical grounding immediately precedes the practical application of the _siddhis_ (supernatural powers) over the physical realm.
