## I. YS III.9 & Hegel: The Concept as Such

**Sūtra III.9 Alignment:**
_vyutthāna-nirodha-saṁskārayor abhibhava-prādurbhāvau nirodha-kṣaṇa-cittānvayo nirodha-pariṇāmaḥ_
(The transformation of restriction is the mind's intersection with the moment of restriction, wherein the latent impressions of emergence are subdued and the impressions of restriction manifest.)

### 1. The Faculty of the Concept

Hegel introduces the "Concept as such" as the faculty of the understanding (_Manas_), which is the faculty for the cognition of the general. We must not take the concept merely as abstractly determined; rather, the understanding is the faculty of the concept _as such_.

In Patañjali, this corresponds to the _Nirodha-pariṇāma_ (restraining transformation). The mind stops producing outward-facing judgments ("emergence/outgoing") and turns back into its own formal capability. The "moment of restriction" (_nirodha-kṣaṇa_) is the precise moment the Concept stops looking at Essence and grasps itself as Concept.

### 2. The Dialectic of Positedness

Hegel explains that the difference and determinations the concept gives itself constitute "positedness." In the concept, this positedness is identical with being-in-and-for-itself. Each moment is the whole concept.

Patañjali describes this geometrically via the _saṁskāras_ (latent impressions). The outgoing (_vyutthāna_) impressions are subdued, and the restrictive (_nirodha_) impressions manifest. The mind posits restriction against emergence. The mind's connection (_anvaya_) with this moment signifies that the Concept is relating only to its own positedness.

## II. YS III.10 & Hegel: The Universal and Particular

**Sūtra III.10 Alignment:**
_tasya praśānta-vāhitā saṁskārāt_
(Its peaceful flow comes by virtue of that restrictive latent impression.)

### 1. The Pure Universal

Hegel defines the subject at this stage as the pure concept, or the determination of universality. Because it is a totality, its universality or pure identical self-reference is essentially a determining and distinguishing.

This pure identical self-reference is the _praśānta-vāhitā_ (peaceful flow) of the mind. The mind is a continuous flow, but because it is completely dominated by the impression of restriction (_saṁskārāt_), it operates as a pure universal, undisturbed by external objectification.

### 2. The Determinate Concept

Hegel notes that the pure universal concept is also only a determinate or particular concept that takes its place alongside others. The concept determines itself immediately as being only the universal against the distinctness of the moments. It is posited as a particular concept distinct from others.

This sets the stage for Singularity. The concept reflects itself out of difference into absolute negativity, stepping out of its identity into otherness to become Judgment (which maps to the following Sūtra, III.11).
