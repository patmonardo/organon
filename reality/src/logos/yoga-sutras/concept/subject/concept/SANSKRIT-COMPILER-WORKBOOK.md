# Concept/Subject/Concept - SANSKRIT COMPILER WORKBOOK

## Purpose

Establishing the baseline Sanskrit philology and conceptual analysis for Yoga Sūtras III.9 and III.10, which govern the _Nirodha-pariṇāma_ (the transformation of restriction). This corresponds to the emergence of the formal Concept.

---

## 1. YS III.9: The Restraining Transformation

### I. Text and Translation

**Devanāgarī:**
व्युत्थाननिरोधसंस्कारयोरभिभवप्रादुर्भावौ निरोधक्षणचित्तान्वयो निरोधपरिणामः ॥ ३.९॥

**Transliteration:**
vyutthāna-nirodha-saṁskārayor abhibhava-prādurbhāvau nirodha-kṣaṇa-cittānvayo nirodha-pariṇāmaḥ || 3.9 ||

**Translation:**
The transformation of restriction (_nirodha-pariṇāmaḥ_) is the mind's intersection (_citta-anvayaḥ_) with the moment of restriction (_nirodha-kṣaṇa_), wherein the latent impressions (_saṁskārayoḥ_) of emergence/outgoing activity (_vyutthāna_) are subdued (_abhibhava_) and the impressions of restriction (_nirodha_) manifest (_prādurbhāvau_).

### II. Philological Breakdown

- **Vyutthāna**: "Rising up," "emergence." The externalizing, objectward movement of the mind. The ordinary state of fluctuating attention.
- **Nirodha**: "Restriction," "cessation," "control." The turning inward, the suppression of externalizing fluctuations.
- **Saṁskārayoḥ**: "Of the latent impressions" or "of the subliminal activators." The deep unconscious tendencies that drive cognition.
- **Abhibhava-prādurbhāvau**: "Subjugation (abhibhava) and manifestation (prādurbhāva)." The specific dialectical mechanism of change. One force goes down as the other comes up.
- **Nirodha-kṣaṇa-cittānvayaḥ**: "The mind's connection/intersection (anvaya) with the moment (kṣaṇa) of restriction." The precise temporal locus where the transformation occurs.
- **Nirodha-pariṇāmaḥ**: "The transformation into restriction." The first of the three inner transformations of Samyama.

## 2. YS III.10: The Peaceful Flow

### I. Text and Translation

**Devanāgarī:**
तस्य प्रशान्तवाहिता संस्कारात्॥ ३.१०॥

**Transliteration:**
tasya praśānta-vāhitā saṁskārāt || 3.10 ||

**Translation:**
Its [the mind's] peaceful flow (_praśānta-vāhitā_) comes by virtue of that [restrictive] latent impression (_saṁskārāt_).

### II. Philological Breakdown

- **Tasya**: "Its" or "of that," referring to the mind (_citta_) from the previous sutra.
- **Praśānta-vāhitā**: "A calm/peaceful (praśānta) continuous flow (vāhitā)." The state of mind when the restrictive impressions have completely saturated the cognitive stream. It is a dynamic state (a flow), but perfectly smooth.
- **Saṁskārāt**: "From/because of the latent impression(s)."

---

## 3. Systemic Analysis

This pivotal sequence details the exact ontological mechanics of entering deep meditative focus (_Samyama_).

### The Dialectic of Impressions

Patañjali introduces a physics of the unconscious. The mind is a battleground of competing _saṁskāras_ (subliminal tendencies). In a normal state, the _vyutthāna_ (outgoing) saṁskāras are dominant, forcing the mind toward objects.

The _nirodha-pariṇāma_ is strictly defined as an inflection point. It is not an absolute void, but a continuous moment (_kṣaṇa_) where the outgoing tendency is dialectically subdued by an emerging tendency of "restriction." This is a highly formal operation: the mind makes an object out of the restriction of objects.

### The Dynamic Stillness

Sūtra III.10 clarifies that this restriction is not a static, dead state. Because it relies on the continuous generation of "restrictive saṁskāras," the mind achieves a _praśānta-vāhitā_—a serene, continuous flow. The mind is still "moving," but it is moving entirely within its own formal determination of restriction. This marks the transition from outward object-seeking (Essence) to formal inward self-reference (The Concept).
