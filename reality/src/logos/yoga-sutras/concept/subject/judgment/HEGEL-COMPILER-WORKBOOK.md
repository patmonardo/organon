## I. YS III.11 & Hegel: The Judgment

**Sūtra III.11 Alignment:**
_sarvārthatā-ekāgratayoḥ kṣayodayau cittasya samādhi-pariṇāmaḥ_
(The destruction of all-pointedness and the rise of one-pointedness is the mind's transformation of absorption.)

### 1. The Diremption of the Concept

Hegel states that Judgment is the determinateness of the concept posited in the concept itself. The original, pure concept (Universality) is an abstract totality. To develop, it must posit its determinations over against each other. It must divide itself.

Patañjali describes this via the _Samādhi-pariṇāma_. The mind takes the tranquil universal flow and forcefully differentiates it. It encounters _sarvārthatā_ (all-pointedness, or the scattered abstraction of immediate existence) and explicitly negates it (_kṣaya_), while simultaneously positing _ekāgratā_ (one-pointedness, a specific, determinate focus) (_udaya_).

### 2. Ahamkāra as the Power of Judgment

The `samyama-greater-varttika` identifies Judgment with _Ahamkāra_ (the determinative power/the egoic partitioner). Hegel calls judgment the subjective positing of determinate concepts, where subject and predicate stand independently.

_Samādhi-pariṇāma_ is not the final resting point; it is the active _process_ of absorption. The mind is actively judging: "This (the one point) is what matters; that (the multi-pointedness) must be destroyed." The mind is subsuming the chaotic particulars (_sarvārthatā_) under the general focal point (_ekāgratā_). Like the Hegelian Judgment, the moments (the old scattered state and the new focused state) are connected, but they still retain a degree of temporal tension or transition. The unity is posited, but it is not yet fully identical to its content.
