# Concept/Subject/Judgment - HEGEL IDEA WORKBOOK

## The Demonstration (_Varttika_): The Diremption of the Concept

Fichte’s principle of constructive genesis explains _why_ the pure Light must break itself to know itself. Patañjali maps this as the destruction of all-pointedness (_sarvārthatā_) and the rising of one-pointedness (_ekāgratā_). Hegel’s _Science of Logic_ provides the rigorous transcendental demonstration of how this _Samādhi-pariṇāma_ structurally necessitates the division of the Subjective Concept into the formal Judgment (_Urteil_), which translates literally as the "original division."

**1. The Judgment as the Concept’s Diremption**
Hegel states that the Concept as such (the Universal from YS III.9-10) is an abstract totality. To develop, it must posit its determinations over against each other. It must divide itself. This precisely mirrors Patañjali's _Samādhi-pariṇāma_. The mind takes the tranquil universal flow and forcefully differentiates it. The universal Concept descends into determinateness, separating out the subject from the predicate. Judgment is the determinateness of the concept posited _in_ the concept itself.

**2. The Negation of the Immediate Manifold**
Hegel shows that Judgment is not just linking two readymade things; it is a movement that negates abstract, immediate existence. _Sarvārthatā_ (all-pointedness) represents the immediate, unposited manifold—the scattered objects of reflection. The Concept explicitly negates this abstraction (_kṣaya_) in order to posit a formal, essential determination (_ekāgratā_ or one-pointedness) as the predicate. The "one point" is not just another object among the many; it is the universal concept asserting itself as the truth _of_ the many.

**3. The Temporal Tension of the Copula**
The _Samādhi-pariṇāma_ is characterized by a specific temporal mechanic: _kṣaya-udayau_ (destruction and rising). The fading of the scattered state is strictly coupled with the emergence of the focused state. In Hegel’s analysis of the Judgment, the "is" (the copula) holds the subject and predicate together while they simultaneously resist each other. They are distinct moments possessing self-subsistence.

The _Samādhi_ is the _process_ of absorption because the mind is actively executing this copula. It is actively judging: "This (the one point) is what matters; that (the multi-pointedness) must be destroyed." The mind is subsuming the chaotic particulars under the general focal point. Because the moments are connected but retain a degree of transition (destruction and rising), the unity is posited, but it is not yet fully identical to its content. The Judgment is inherently unstable; it demands its own resolution into the Syllogism (YS III.12), where the transition is finally sublated into absolute identity.
