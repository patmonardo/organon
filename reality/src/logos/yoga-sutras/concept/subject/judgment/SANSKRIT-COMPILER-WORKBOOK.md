# Concept/Subject/Judgment - SANSKRIT COMPILER WORKBOOK

## Purpose

Establishing the baseline Sanskrit philology and conceptual analysis for Yoga Sūtra III.11, which governs the _Samādhi-pariṇāma_ (the transformation of absorption). This corresponds to the phase of Judgment (the positing of the determinate concepts) in the Subjective Logic.

---

## 1. YS III.11: The Transformation of Absorption

### I. Text and Translation

**Devanāgarī:**
सर्वार्थतैकाग्रतयोः क्षयोदयौ चित्तस्य समाधिपरिणामः ॥ ३.११॥

**Transliteration:**
sarvārthatā-ekāgratayoḥ kṣayodayau cittasya samādhi-pariṇāmaḥ || 3.11 ||

**Translation:**
The destruction (_kṣaya_) of all-pointedness (_sarvārthatā_) and the rise (_udaya_) of one-pointedness (_ekāgratayoḥ_) is the mind's (_cittasya_) transformation of absorption (_samādhi-pariṇāmaḥ_).

### II. Philological Breakdown

- **Sarvārthatā**: "Multi-pointedness," "all-objectness." The state of the mind when its attention is indiscriminately scattered across multiple, disparate objects. The prefix _sarva_ means "all" or "every;" _artha_ means "object" or "meaning."
- **Ekāgratayoḥ**: "Of one-pointedness." The state of the mind when it is focused on a single, singular target or principle.
- **Kṣaya-udayau**: "Destruction and rising." This is the identical dialectical mechanism described in III.9 (_abhibhava-prādurbhāvau_), applied to a new set of conditions. The fading of the scattered state is strictly coupled with the emergence of the focused state.
- **Cittasya**: "Of the mind."
- **Samādhi-pariṇāmaḥ**: "Transformation into samādhi (cognitive absorption)." The second of the three inner transformations.

---

## 2. Systemic Analysis

If the first transformation (_nirodha_, YS III.9) restricts the outward flow to create a peaceful universal platform, the second transformation (_samādhi_, YS III.11) begins to formally structure that platform.

### From Universality to Determination

The initial tranquil flow (_praśānta-vāhitā_) is a pure universal. However, a mind entirely without an object is an empty abstraction. The mind must focus on _something_ to complete its cognitive realization. Therefore, the mind executes the _Samādhi-pariṇāma_.

This transformation occurs by collapsing "all-pointedness." The mind ceases to treat reality as an aggregate of disconnected "things out there" (_sarvārthatā_). Instead, it posits a singular determinateness (_ekāgratā_).

### The Formal Judgment

In logical terms, "all-pointedness" is the unformed aggregate. To move to "one-pointedness" is to isolate a specific determination. The mind _judges_—it breaks the universal open and posits a particular, determinate concept (the "one point") against the background of the universal. The mind separates the Subject from the Predicate to establish a focused relation between them.

This state, _Samādhi-pariṇāma_, represents the transitional stage where the mind is actively engaged in the process of converging on unity. The unity is not yet absolute (that comes in III.12); here, the unity is _posited_ by explicitly destroying distraction and raising focus.
