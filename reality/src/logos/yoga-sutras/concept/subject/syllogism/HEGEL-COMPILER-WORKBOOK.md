## I. YS III.12 & Hegel: The Syllogism

**Sūtra III.12 Alignment:**
_tataḥ punaḥ śāntoditau tulya-pratyayau cittasya ekāgratā-pariṇāmaḥ_
(Then again, when the subsiding and arising cognitions are identical, that is the mind's transformation of one-pointedness.)

### 1. The Restored Unity

Hegel defines the Syllogism as the result of the restoration of the concept in the judgment, and consequently the unity and truth of the two (Concept and Judgment). In the judgment, the moments were posited as self-subsisting extremes. In the syllogism, their determinate unity is posited.

Patañjali describes this sublation of extremes in the _Ekāgratā-pariṇāma_. The "extremes" in the cognitive sequence are the cognition that is passing away (_śānta_, the past) and the cognition that is arising (_udita_, the present/future). In ordinary cognition (and in the prior Judgment phase), these two extremes differ in content. But here, the extremes are made perfectly identical (_tulya-pratyayau_).

### 2. The Middle Term (Buddhi)

Hegel's syllogism connects the immediate extremes via a mediating middle term. The `samyama-greater-varttika` identifies the Syllogism with _Buddhi_ (Reason)—the faculty for derivation from principles.

Because the arising thought and the subsiding thought are strictly identical, the mind's focus acts as the perfect Hegelian Middle Term. It mediates the transition of Time itself without introducing any alterity. The contradictory relation that occurs in the formal sequence sublates itself.

Hegel notes that when this mediatedness is reflected into itself (the Syllogism of Necessity), it determines the extremes as totalities. The concept attains its truth and steps forth out of subjectivity into objectivity. For Patañjali, this is the perfection of _Samyama_—the mind is now fully prepared to map its completely synthesized subjective architecture onto the external objective world (attaining the _siddhis_ or perfections that follow in the rest of Chapter 3).
