# Concept/Subject/Syllogism - SANSKRIT COMPILER WORKBOOK

## Purpose

Establishing the baseline Sanskrit philology and conceptual analysis for Yoga Sūtra III.12, which governs the _Ekāgratā-pariṇāma_ (the transformation of one-pointedness). This corresponds to the phase of the Syllogism (the restored unity of the concept in its totality) in the Subjective Logic.

---

## 1. YS III.12: The Transformation of One-Pointedness

### I. Text and Translation

**Devanāgarī:**
ततः पुनः शान्तोदितौ तुल्यप्रत्ययौ चित्तस्यैकाग्रतापरिणामः ॥ ३.१२॥

**Transliteration:**
tataḥ punaḥ śāntoditau tulya-pratyayau cittasya ekāgratā-pariṇāmaḥ || 3.12 ||

**Translation:**
Then again (_tataḥ punaḥ_), when the subsiding (_śānta_) and arising (_uditau_) cognitions (_pratyayau_) are strictly identical (_tulya_), that is the mind's (_cittasya_) transformation into one-pointedness (_ekāgratā-pariṇāmaḥ_).

### II. Philological Breakdown

- **Tataḥ punaḥ**: "Then again," or "furthermore." Indicating the final stage in this three-part dialectic of Samyama transformations.
- **Śānta-uditau**: "The pacified/past (śānta) and the arisen/present (udita)." Referring specifically to the sequential moments of cognition.
- **Tulya-pratyayau**: "Identical/equal (tulya) cognitions (pratyayau)." The content of the thought that just passed away is mathematically identical to the content of the thought that currently exists.
- **Cittasya**: "Of the mind."
- **Ekāgratā-pariṇāmaḥ**: "Transformation into one-pointedness." The culmination of the interior sequence.

---

## 2. Systemic Analysis

This is the perfection of focus. The previous stage (_Samādhi-pariṇāma_, III.11) was the _process_ of destroying distraction and raising focus. The mind was judging between two different states: a scattered past and a focused future.

### The Absolute Identity of the Flow

In the _Ekāgratā-pariṇāma_, the internal difference between subject and predicate entirely collapses. The mind is no longer fighting off distraction. Instead, a specific cognition arises (_udita_). It stays for a moment, and then subsides into the past (_śānta_). The cognition that immediately replaces it is _exactly identical_ (_tulya_).

Because the content of time $T_1$ and time $T_2$ are perfectly identical, the mind experiences an unbroken, seamless continuum. The formal sequence of time (which implies difference and change) is effectively sublated into a spatialized unity.

### The Restored Unity

The mind has achieved a perfect syllogism. The extremes (the past moment and the future moment, or the Subject and the Predicate) have been mediated by an absolutely identical middle term (the specific object of concentration). The struggle of Judgment is over, and the Concept is restored to its full, concrete totality. The mind is structurally "one-pointed" without having to exert force to maintain it. This marks the full structural realization of _Samyama_.
