### Entry heg-iv-019 — Dissolution of Appearance

Span:

- sourceFile: `src/logos/hegel-logic/dharma-greater-varttika.tex`
- lineStart: 5259
- lineEnd: 5280

Summary:
The object-status of the mind (_dṛśyatvāt_) prevents it from being self-luminous. This corresponds to Hegel's "Dissolution of Appearance," where the world of appearance relates to the world-in-itself (Puruṣa) as an inverted opposition. The mind, being merely an appearing property/instrument within the world of appearance, cannot be the positive illuminating source itself; what is positive in the world-in-itself (pure knowing) appears only as a reflected, dependent negativity in the object-mind.

Key points: (KeyPoint)

- k1. The mind is an instrument of appearance, not the pure underlying world-in-itself.
- k2. Claiming self-luminosity for the mind confuses the world of appearance for the world-in-itself.
- k3. The dissolution reveals that the true inner ground (Puruṣa) is the direct inverse of the outer appearance layer.

### Entry heg-iv-020 — The Essential Relation

Span:

- sourceFile: `src/logos/hegel-logic/dharma-greater-varttika.tex`
- lineStart: 5389
- lineEnd: 5410

Summary:
The prohibition of determining both seer and seen simultaneously (_eka-samaye_) formalizes Hegel's transition into "The Essential Relation." The truth of Appearance is precisely this structural Relation, where reflection into an other is simultaneously reflection into itself. However, because the relation contains mutually indifferent sides (subject/object as appearance), trying to hold them as absolutely identical within a single mental act collapses the necessary difference that defines the Relation.

Key points: (KeyPoint)

- k1. The cognitive act cannot simultaneously be the immediate existence and the reflected existence.
- k2. The sides of the Relation are structurally distinct; uniting them cleanly requires the absolute standpoint, not the dualistic _citta_.

### Entry heg-iv-021 — The Relation of Whole and Parts

Span:

- sourceFile: `src/logos/hegel-logic/dharma-greater-varttika.tex`
- lineStart: 5499
- lineEnd: 5520

Summary:
The refutation of "minds observing minds" (_atiprasaṅga_) aligns specifically with Hegel's first phase of the Essential Relation: "The Relation of Whole and Parts." Treating one mind as the observer of another reduces subjective unity to an infinite mechanical regress of distinct abstract parts lacking a true Whole. This infinite layering (the regress) shatters the essential self-subsistence of the relation, proving that a higher-order overarching Whole (the absolute Witness) is required rather than just a linear horizontal chain of observing parts.

Key points: (KeyPoint)

- k1. A linear regress of minds observing minds is the mechanical fallacy of Parts attempting to ground themselves without a Whole.
- k2. The confusion of memory streams points to the collapse of identity that occurs when the Whole-Part relation is degraded into infinite Parts without an organizing invariant.
