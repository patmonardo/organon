# The Judgment of the Concept — Distillation

### §1. The Concept Now Judges Reality by an Ought

**Lines:** 1–92

**Distillation:**
The judgment of the concept is the first judgment that contains true adjudication. The concept stands over against actuality as an ought to which the fact may or may not correspond. Its predicates such as good, bad, true, right, and fitting no longer express mere existence, reflection, or necessity in the earlier sense, but explicit conformity or non-conformity of reality to the concept itself. For that reason this judgment is genuinely objective, even though it seems at first to reintroduce subjectivity.

**Structural Moves:**

- **True adjudication:** Reality is measured against the concept.
- **Ought and actuality:** The concept stands as norm over the fact.
- **Objective judgment:** This is not mere subjective opinion but conceptual truth.

### §2. The Assertoric Judgment States the Fact but Leaves the Connection External

**Lines:** 93–165

**Distillation:**
The assertoric judgment says of a concrete subject that it is good, bad, right, and so on, but the ground of this predication is not yet posited within the judgment itself. The subject contains both its universal ought and its immediate constitution, yet these are only externally connected. As a result, the assertoric judgment rests on subjective assurance. The copula remains immediate, and an opposite assertion can always be made with equal right.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Immediate assertion:** The judgment declares a verdict.
- **External grounding:** The basis of the verdict is not yet explicit.
- **Subjective assurance:** Opposed assertions remain equally possible.

### §3. The Problematic Judgment Posits Contingency in the Subject Itself

**Lines:** 166–259

**Distillation:**
The problematic judgment internalizes what was merely external in the assertoric form. The issue is no longer whether we happen to attach a predicate to the subject, but whether the subject in its very constitution does or does not conform to its concept. The subject is thereby divided into its ought and its immediate existence, and this division is recognized as the ground of the judgment itself. The problematic form is thus more truthful than the assertoric, because it explicitly posits contingency where contingency really lies: in the subject's immediate constitution.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Internalized contingency:** The problematic element is placed in the subject itself.
- **Division of subject:** Ought and actuality are distinguished within one fact.
- **Ground of judgment:** The subject contains the basis of its own evaluation.

---

### §4. The Apodictic Judgment Achieves Objective Correspondence and Becomes Syllogism

**Lines:** 260–374

**Distillation:**
The apodictic judgment is the truth of the judgment of the concept. Here the subject includes both its universal essence and the constitution that grounds whether the predicate truly belongs to it. Subject and predicate therefore correspond and contain the same concept. The copula is no longer the abstract is, but the determinate ground connecting them. Because that connecting ground itself contains the full content of subject and predicate, the form of judgment passes away. The unity of the concept that had been divided across the judgment is recovered as replete copula, and by virtue of that repletion the judgment becomes syllogism.

**Structural Moves:**

- **Objective correspondence:** Subject and predicate share one concept.
- **Grounded copula:** The connection becomes determinate ground.
- **Passing away of judgment:** The divided form can no longer stand.
- **Transition:** Judgment of the concept becomes syllogism.
